Dynamite Headdy

&#91; b &#93;

is a 1994  platform

video game  developed by  Treasure  and published by  Sega  for the  Sega Genesis . The game follows a puppet named Headdy in his efforts to stop an evil puppet king from taking over his world. Headdy can throw his head at enemies to defeat them and use it to pull himself to various areas and move objects. The player can find a wide variety of "heads" which act as  power-ups  that provide different effects and alter gameplay.

Dynamite Headdy  received positive reviews from critics, who highlighted the game's originality and creativity in distinguishing it among other games in the platform genre. They also commended Treasure for demonstrating their technical expertise with impressive graphical effects and building on the success of their earlier game

Gunstar Heroes

(1993).  Dynamite Headdy  has been cited as one of the best games on the Genesis, and has since been included in multiple  compilations  of Genesis games. An  8-bit  port was released for  Game Gear , which itself served as the basis for a  Master System  port released in  Brazil .

Gameplay

[

edit

]

Headdy fighting with Trouble Bruin early in the game

Dynamite Headdy  is a  platform game  in which the player controls Headdy, a puppet with a detachable head.

&#91; 7 &#93;

The story follows Headdy in his adventures to save his world from the evil puppet King Dark Demon, who is transforming all the puppets of the world into his evil minions. To succeed, Headdy must overcome the King's army, which features the likes of Trouble Bruin

&#91; c &#93;

and the key masters which serve as stage bosses.

&#91; 8 &#93;

Headdy can jump and throw his head in any direction to attack enemies. His head can also be used to grab hooks and pull himself up to other platforms.

&#91; 9 &#93;

&#58;&#8202;6&#8202;

Headdy's health is represented by a spotlight in the corner of the screen which changes colors. If the player does not pick up restorative items to heal themselves after suffering damage from enemies, they will lose a life.

&#91; 9 &#93;

&#58;&#8202;7&#8202;

During his adventure, Headdy will find  power-ups  which change the design and function of his head. These 17 power-ups range from increasing damage, such as spinning fireballs around his head, to providing invincibility, restoring health, increasing speed, etc.

&#91; 2 &#93;

Some of these power-ups last for only a short time. Other than heads, the player can also find health restoratives, extra points, and extra lives throughout the levels. By finding the Liberty Head, players can enter a Bonus Stage in which the player must shoot basketballs into the correct hoops.

&#91; 9 &#93;

&#58;&#8202;10–13&#8202;

Unlike other platform games where falling into a bottomless pit results instant death, if Headdy falls off, he will bounce back up which allows the player to put him back on a platform, though Headdy will still sustain some damage.

Development and release

[

edit

]

After developing

Gunstar Heroes  ,  Treasure  was divided into four teams to work on four separate projects:

Alien Soldier  ,

Light Crusader  ,

Yū Yū Hakusho Makyō Tōitsusen  , and  Dynamite Headdy .

&#91; 10 &#93;

Development of  Dynamite Headdy  started in August 1993.

&#91; 11 &#93;

Inspiration for the game came from Koichi Kimura, who wanted to work on an original project. Up to this point in his five-year career, Kimura had only worked on projects using the pre-existing works of others.

&#91; 12 &#93;

Kimura lead development,

&#91; 12 &#93;

and president of Treasure, Masato Maegawa, served as head programmer.

&#91; 13 &#93;

Kimura designed  Dynamite Headdy  to be commercially viable.

&#91; 12 &#93;

He wanted the main character to attack with a part of its body, so he felt a puppet that could throw its head would be appealing. Not wanting Headdy to be a stereotypical lone hero, Kimura added support characters that appear in every stage.

&#91; 12 &#93;

The game's visual design was inspired by  Western animation  and the work of film director  Terry Gilliam .

&#91; 12 &#93;

After deciding to publish a localized version for overseas markets, Maegawa was told by  Sega  of America to make the game more difficult. This was because Sega did not want customers completing the game too easily on rental. Maegawa happily obliged, and the North American release was made about twice as difficult.

&#91; 10 &#93;

In addition to changes in difficulty, most of the dialogue in the game was removed, and several of the character and boss sprites were changed.

&#91; 14 &#93;

Dynamite Headdy  was originally released in Japan on August 5, 1994 for the  Sega Genesis , with North American and European releases following in September and October respectively.

&#91; 8 &#93;

&#91; 15 &#93;

&#91; 16 &#93;

A port was released for the  Game Gear , and a  Master System  port based on the Game Gear version was released exclusively in Brazil.

&#91; 14 &#93;

&#91; 17 &#93;

A  32X  conversion was planned but canceled.

&#91; 18 &#93;

The game was included along with  Gunstar Heroes  and  Alien Soldier  in

Sega Ages 2500 : Gunstar Heroes Treasure Box , a compilation released for the  PlayStation 2  in 2006 in Japan. The compilation includes all versions of  Dynamite Headdy  released on all three Sega platforms across all regions.

&#91; 14 &#93;

&#91; 17 &#93;

&#91; 19 &#93;

The game saw a digital re-release on the  Wii  via the  Virtual Console  in July 2007.

&#91; 20 &#93;

It was also included in

Sonic's Ultimate Genesis Collection

for  Xbox 360  and  PlayStation 3 , and was released on  Windows  through  Steam  on May 2, 2012.

&#91; 14 &#93;

&#91; 21 &#93;

In 2018, it was included in the multi-platform compilation

Sega Genesis Classics  .

&#91; 22 &#93;

In 2021,  Dynamite Headdy  was added to the  Nintendo Classics  service.

&#91; 23 &#93;

Dynamite Headdy  received critical acclaim,

&#91; 14 &#93;

with some believing it to be one of the best games on the Genesis.

&#91; 24 &#93;

&#91; 28 &#93;

&#91; 6 &#93;

Journalists repeatedly highlighted the game's creativity as a strong point.

&#91; 16 &#93;

&#91; 24 &#93;

&#91; 28 &#93;

&#91; 29 &#93;

Rik Skews of

Computer and Video Games

said it "breathes new life" into the platform genre and stood up to the likes of the

Sonic

series.

&#91; 16 &#93;

The staff at

Mean Machines Sega

found the game innovative and liked how each stage introduced new concepts to keep gameplay fresh.

&#91; 28 &#93;

Tim Tucker at

GamesMaster

believed that Treasure was doing more innovation in the genre than any of its rivals.

&#91; 27 &#93;

In addition to the game's originality, critics praised Treasure's programming expertise and  Dynamite Headdy' s technical prowess.

&#91; 24 &#93;

&#91; 27 &#93;

&#91; 28 &#93;

The staff of

Electronic Gaming Monthly

complimented the game's visual effects and colorful stages.

&#91; 24 &#93;

Tucker said " Dynamite Headdy  is full of classic Treasure overstatement" and cited the impressive colors, big explosions, large sprites, and rapidly changing backgrounds—which all together were perhaps a little too overwhelming.

&#91; 27 &#93;

The  Mean Machines  staff believed that  Dynamite Headdy  followed in the footsteps of Treasure's previous games and therefore confirmed the studio's technical expertise.

&#91; 28 &#93;

The gameplay drew some criticism, with Tucker finding it somewhat disjointed and "patchy" and  Mean Machines  stating that it takes a few stages to truly find its footing.

&#91; 27 &#93;

&#91; 28 &#93;

Critics found the Game Gear port to be well done given the system's less powerful technology.

&#91; 6 &#93;

&#91; 31 &#93;

&#91; 25 &#93;

Electronic Gaming Monthly  staff called it one of the best portable action games.

&#91; 25 &#93;

Richard Leadbetter of

Sega Magazine

believed that since much of the Genesis version's playability came from the system's technical capabilities, the Game Gear version suffers in that sense, but still believed Treasure was able to create a fun 8-bit port.

&#91; 6 &#93;

In retrospective reviews of the Genesis version,  Dynamite Headdy  has continued to receive considerable praise. Critics agreed that the game is excellent despite not receiving the same legendary status as some of Treasure's other works such as  Gunstar Heroes ,

Ikaruga  , and

Radiant Silvergun  .

&#91; 7 &#93;

&#91; 32 &#93;

&#91; 33 &#93;

Lucas M. Thomas of

IGN

said the game is overload on the eyes, but it makes sense with respect to Treasure's chaotic design.

&#91; 7 &#93;

Frank Provo of

GameSpot

noted the game's impressive graphical effects, large bosses, and bizarre premise providing the same level of excitement as  Gunstar Heroes .

&#91; 32 &#93;

Damien McFarren of

Nintendo Life

called it one of the best Treasure games of the Genesis era and one of the best platformers on the system.

&#91; 33 &#93;
