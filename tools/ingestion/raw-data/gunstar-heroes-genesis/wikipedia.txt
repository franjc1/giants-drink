Gunstar Heroes

&#91; b &#93;

is a 1993  run and gun video game  developed by  Treasure  and published by  Sega  for the  Sega Genesis . It was Treasure's first developed video game. The premise is centered around a pair of characters, the Gunstars, in their efforts to stop an evil empire from recovering four powerful gems. The characters can fire guns and perform a series of acrobatic maneuvers to fight enemies across each stage. There are four weapons which can be combined with one another to create different shot types.

Development on  Gunstar Heroes  began among a team of staff working at  Konami  in 1991. Following an unwillingness of Konami to embrace their original game ideas, the team quit in 1992 and formed Treasure to see their project through. The team wanted to develop their game for the Genesis because of the system's powerful  Motorola 68000  microprocessor.  Sega  initially rejected their proposal, but later granted approval after they worked for Sega for several months on

McDonald's Treasure Land Adventure

(1993). Treasure worked on both games in parallel, and released  Gunstar Heroes  as their first game in 1993.

Gunstar Heroes  was a critical success, being praised for its frantic action and advanced graphics. It helped establish Treasure's place in the industry, and introduced numerous design conventions which would become characteristic of their later work such as large bosses and a unique sense of humor. It was re-released several times, including dedicated ports to the  Game Gear  and  Nintendo 3DS , and received  a sequel  on the  Game Boy Advance . In retrospect, it is considered one of the best action games of the  16-bit era , and one of the  best video games of all time  by several publications.

Gameplay

[

edit

]

The player runs to the right as they fire the machine gun.

Gunstar Heroes  is a  run and gun  game which is played from a  side-scrolling  perspective similar to

Contra  .

&#91; 6 &#93;

The game can be played in  single-player , or  cooperatively  with a partner. The players take on the role of Gunstar Red and Gunstar Blue as they battle with an evil empire for control over a set of powerful gems.

&#91; 7 &#93;

The game features seven stages, of which the first four can be played in any order.

&#91; 7 &#93;

The stage formats vary. While some feature a left-to-right format, others include the player riding in a mine cart along walls, fighting enemies on a helicopter, or playing a board game.

&#91; 7 &#93;

&#91; 8 &#93;

Completing a level grants the player an extension to their maximum health, known as vitality in the game.

&#91; 7 &#93;

When starting the game, the player can choose either a free or fixed firing stance; the free stance allows the player to move in the direction they are firing, while the fixed stance immobilizes the character when shooting.

&#91; 8 &#93;

The player also has a choice of starting weapon. There are four shot types in the game: a homing shot, lightning blaster, flamethrower, and machine gun. Every weapon has its strengths and weaknesses, and can be swapped with others from item drops.

&#91; 9 &#93;

The weapons can be combined with each other to produce unique shot types. For example, the homing shot can be combined with the machine gun to add a homing effect to the latter, or two lightning shots can be combined to create a more powerful lightning gun.

&#91; 9 &#93;

In addition to firing their weapon, the player characters can perform a series of acrobatic maneuvers including jumping, sliding, and grabbing and throwing enemies.

&#91; 7 &#93;

Development

[

edit

]

In 1991, several  Konami  employees led by programmer Masato Maegawa began holding planning sessions at coffee shops for an original game.

&#91; 10 &#93;

&#91; 11 &#93;

They were establishing early concepts for an action shooting game, a genre they were familiar with.

&#91; 11 &#93;

They pitched their idea to Konami, but were told it would not sell and it was rejected.

&#91; 12 &#93;

Maegawa and his team were growing frustrated with the industry's reliance on sequels to established franchises and console conversions of arcade games to generate revenue.

&#91; 12 &#93;

They felt Konami had fallen into this pattern as a large company, growing reliant on sequels in their

Castlevania

and

Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles

series.

&#91; 12 &#93;

Maegawa and his team felt consumers wanted original games,

&#91; 12 &#93;

and so in 1992, they left Konami and established  Treasure  to continue development on their unique game.

&#91; 13 &#93;

Treasure believed the  Motorola 68000  microprocessor in the  Sega Genesis  was best suited for the action games they wanted to make.

Treasure wanted to develop their game for the  Sega Genesis .

&#91; 12 &#93;

Maegawa liked the platform because of its  Motorola 68000  microprocessor, which he felt was easier to program for than the  Super NES , and more powerful.

&#91; 12 &#93;

The team decided to approach Sega for a publishing contract.

&#91; 10 &#93;

When they presented their design documents to Sega, they were not granted approval because they lacked a proven track record.

&#91; 11 &#93;

Instead, Sega contracted Treasure to develop

McDonald's Treasure Land Adventure

(1993).

&#91; 11 &#93;

Several months into development, they were granted approval to work on their game.

&#91; 11 &#93;

They were initially using the working title  Lunatic Gunstar , thinking "Lunatic" was a good description of the stimulating action.

&#91; 10 &#93;

Sega of America felt the word held a negative connotation, so rejected it.

&#91; 11 &#93;

The team also considered  Blade Gunner , in homage to

Blade Runner  , but this was also rejected due to copyright restrictions.

&#91; 11 &#93;

Sega of America eventually suggested the word "Heroes", so  Gunstar Heroes  became the final title.

&#91; 11 &#93;

Treasure consisted of around 18 people, most being programmers from Konami.

&#91; 12 &#93;

The staff was split in half to work on both  Gunstar Heroes  and  McDonald's Treasure Land Adventure  in parallel.

&#91; 11 &#93;

&#91; 12 &#93;

The core team behind  Gunstar Heroes  consisted of six people: two programmers, two graphic designers, and two sound programmers.

&#91; 12 &#93;

The staff (known by their nicknames) was composed of main programmer Yaiman, enemy and boss programmer Nami, graphic designers Han and Iuchi, composer Non, and sound effects programmer Murata.

&#91; 10 &#93;

The role of game designer did not exist within Treasure. All game design and planning was undertaken by everyone involved.

&#91; 13 &#93;

The team felt they had more freedom working under Sega than Konami.

&#91; 12 &#93;

Maegawa got approval to add a Treasure logo when the game booted, which he felt was a rare opportunity for developers to receive during that era.

&#91; 11 &#93;

The Genesis's processor made multi-limbed bosses like "Seven Force" possible.

Development of  Gunstar Heroes  lasted around nine to ten months.

&#91; 10 &#93;

&#91; 12 &#93;

It was the team's first experience programming for the Genesis, having come off of programming for the Super NES at Konami.

&#91; 11 &#93;

&#91; 13 &#93;

Programmers Mitsuru Yaida and Hideyuki Suganami previously programmed

Contra III: The Alien Wars

(1992) for the Super NES at Konami.

&#91; 14 &#93;

The team felt the Genesis's processor was more powerful, capable, and friendly to experimentation than other consoles. This led them to consider it more suited for action games and the sophisticated graphical effects they were looking to create.

&#91; 11 &#93;

&#91; 12 &#93;

&#91; 13 &#93;

The team implemented heavy visual effects in an exercise in design experimentation (not an effort to push the hardware).

&#91; 13 &#93;

Some of that experimenting was done with the system's  sprite  rotation and scaling capabilities, which evoked a sense of depth.

&#91; 10 &#93;

&#91; 11 &#93;

&#91; 12 &#93;

The Genesis had its limitations however, as it could only display 64 colors on-screen at once.

&#91; 12 &#93;

The team placed extra effort on coloring to compensate.

&#91; 13 &#93;

Early backgrounds were drawn with a 16 color palette, but they looked desolate, so ultimately two palettes with 32 colors were used.

&#91; 10 &#93;

The team also used programming tricks to make the scenery appear like three to four layers were present, even though the Genesis only supports two.

&#91; 12 &#93;

The team approached  Gunstar Heroes  with an "anything goes" concept, that led to many ambitious ideas being implemented into the final game.

&#91; 10 &#93;

&#91; 11 &#93;

One such concept was the weapon combination mechanic, which was conceived in the early planning stages.

&#91; 13 &#93;

They experimented with weapon attributes until the end of development,

&#91; 13 &#93;

and designed the game so players would continue discovering new weapons and devise unique ways to complete stages.

&#91; 10 &#93;

The processor also made articulated multi-limb enemies possible, like the boss "Seven Force", which was programmed by Nami along with all other bosses.

&#91; 11 &#93;

Since moving around large sprites was difficult due to limited  video RAM , Seven Force was built by combining circles and squares to make one large character.

&#91; 11 &#93;

Maegawa claims the game would not have worked on the Super NES because the boss animations required expanded computing power.

&#91; 12 &#93;

The standard enemy characters, designed by Han, were drawn on the screen by combining a top and bottom sprite, allowing for more animation patterns with lower memory usage.

&#91; 11 &#93;

Han was inspired by the game

Mazin Saga: Mutant Fighter

(1993) to program the enemies manually rather than with mathematical algorithms.

&#91; 15 &#93;

The game was 16  megabits , but compressed to fit under 8.

&#91; 12 &#93;

Release

[

edit

]

Although  McDonald's Treasure Land Adventure  was completed beforehand, Treasure decided to wait and release  Gunstar Heroes  first because they wanted their debut to be an original game.

&#91; 11 &#93;

It was nearly rejected for an American release by Sega of America, only being approved by Mac Senour, the last producer in the reviewing queue after 12 others rejected it. Senour believed the other producers dismissed the game because its small character sprites contrasted with the more popular large sprites at the time. Senour did ask Treasure to change one boss character because it looked too similar to  Adolf Hitler .

&#91; 16 &#93;

Gunstar Heroes  was released in Japan on September 10, 1993,

&#91; 3 &#93;

and released in the West the same month.

&#91; 10 &#93;

Sega underestimated demand in Japan where their initial shipment was only 10,000 units.

&#91; 17 &#93;

Meanwhile, Sega of America also ordered a small initial print run, as they were not typically confident in games from Sega of Japan.

&#91; 16 &#93;

The game was not heavily promoted and spread through speech.

&#91; 18 &#93;

GameFan

particularly enjoyed the game and published the first English interview with Maegawa.

&#91; 18 &#93;

The game shipped around 70,000 copies in Japan, and 200,000 overseas.

&#91; 17 &#93;

An arcade version was also released for the  Sega Mega-Play  arcade board.

&#91; 11 &#93;

Ports and re-releases

[

edit

]

A modified version was released for Sega's  Mega-Play  arcade hardware in 1994. Changes include multiple lives being given per credit (as opposed to a single life in the console version), a 10-second timer which forces the player to make a decision when appropriate, and  continuous play  by inserting additional credits. &#91;

citation needed

&#93;

A  Game Gear  port was developed by  M2 . Sega asked M2 if they wanted to make a Game Gear game after being impressed by their

Gauntlet

port for the Genesis. M2 wanted to port

The Cliffhanger: Edward Randy  , but they were ultimately handed  Gunstar Heroes . Since the Genesis version pushed hardware limitations, a Game Gear port was difficult.

&#91; 19 &#93;

The port had some changes, such as the Dice Palace being removed, and it ran at lower frame rate.

&#91; 10 &#93;

It was released on March 24, 1995.

&#91; 20 &#93;

Sega released  Gunstar Heroes Treasure Box  in 2006 in Japan, a compilation of Treasure games for the  PlayStation 2  as part of their

Sega Ages 2500

series.

&#91; 21 &#93;

Included on the compilation were  Gunstar Heroes ,

Dynamite Headdy

(1994), and

Alien Soldier

(1995).

&#91; 10 &#93;

&#91; 21 &#93;

The games are run through an emulator and include display options for filters and resolutions. The Japanese and international versions were included, along with the Game Gear ports of  Gunstar Heroes  and  Dynamite Headdy , and a Genesis prototype of  Gunstar Heroes .

&#91; 22 &#93;

A gallery is included with scans of the original instruction manuals, concept illustrations, and design documents.

&#91; 23 &#93;

The compilation was released digitally on the  PlayStation Store  in 2012.

&#91; 23 &#93;

The game was ported by M2 to the  Nintendo 3DS  in 2015 as part of Sega's line of

3D Classics  .

&#91; 24 &#93;

Gunstar Heroes  was originally not considered for the series because converting the game's backgrounds into a layered 3D effect was thought to be impossible, but these perceived problems were later overcome.

&#91; 25 &#93;

In addition to supporting  stereoscopic 3D , the game features two new modes. "Gunslinger" mode starts the player with a full arsenal of every weapon type and grants the ability to change shot stance at any time. "Mega Life" mode starts the player with double the normal amount of health.

&#91; 26 &#93;

The game also supports local cooperative play and includes both the Japanese and international versions.

&#91; 24 &#93;

Gunstar Heroes  has also been released on the  Xbox 360 ,

&#91; 27 &#93;

PlayStation 3 ,

&#91; 28 &#93;

Wii  via  Virtual Console ,

&#91; 9 &#93;

and  Windows .

&#91; 29 &#93;

A  Sega Forever  version for mobile devices was released in 2017.

&#91; 7 &#93;

The game was again released via the  Nintendo Classics  service in 2021.

&#91; 30 &#93;

Legacy

[

edit

]

Gunstar Heroes  was listed as one of the best games ever made by several publications.

&#91; 45 &#93;

&#91; 46 &#93;

&#91; 47 &#93;

&#91; 48 &#93;

&#91; 49 &#93;

&#91; 50 &#93;

Critics have called it a "classic" of the 16-bit era,

&#91; 24 &#93;

&#91; 6 &#93;

with  IGN  writing that its pace and speed was "nearly unrivaled" at the time.

&#91; 9 &#93;

Others called it one of the best Genesis games.

&#91; 6 &#93;

&#91; 51 &#93;

&#91; 52 &#93;

Electronic Gaming Monthly

called it "one of the best two-player games ever made."

&#91; 48 &#93;

USgamer  wrote that its chaotic and brash nature made it the "quintessential classic-era Sega game" in contrast to the more gentle offerings from  Nintendo .

&#91; 8 &#93;

Nintendo World Report  called it "an incredible accomplishment, both creatively and technically" for the Genesis.

&#91; 6 &#93;

Both  TouchArcade  and  GameSpy  deemed it one of the best side-scrolling action games ever made.

&#91; 7 &#93;

&#91; 51 &#93;

Being Treasure's debut game,  Gunstar Heroes  helped establish their reputation in the industry.

&#91; 10 &#93;

&#91; 53 &#93;

Retro Gamer  said the game "blew open the state of 2D platform-shooters."

&#91; 10 &#93;

Developers at the time borrowed inspiration for titles like

Vectorman

(1995) and

Shinobi III: Return of the Ninja Master

(1993).

&#91; 54 &#93;

&#91; 55 &#93;

Treasure also started building a following of loyal hardcore fans,

&#91; 56 &#93;

which  USgamer  felt was earned by "a sense of integrity to Treasure's work you don't often see in games of that era."

&#91; 53 &#93;

1UP.com

wrote that between  Gunstar Heroes  and their other Genesis games, Treasure earned a reputation as a "master of hardcore action gaming."

&#91; 18 &#93;

Many of the design conventions Treasure used in  Gunstar Heroes  would return and became characteristic of their later work, such as a quirky sense of humor and action taken to a level of absurdity.

&#91; 18 &#93;

A sequel was released for the Game Boy Advance in 2005,

Gunstar Super Heroes  .

&#91; 10 &#93;
