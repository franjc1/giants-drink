Mega Man 6

is the sixth adventure of Mega Man, sent forth by Capcom to free the world from the diabolical Mr. X, who is most certainly not Dr. Wily with a fake beard, don't be absurd.

The game came very close to being a Japan-exclusive; it had to end up being published by Nintendo themselves in the US, due in part to the fact that the Robot Masters were designed in a contest by Capcom and two of them were by North Americans. It was also not released in Europe until 2013 in digital form.

Prerelease Info

Sprite Viewer/Sound Test

To do:  Figure out how to properly reactivate the in-game debug menu that leads here.

There is a sprite viewer and sound test combo menu that can be accessed through  Game Genie  code

EZKKIVKG+LLVKATOL+AXEGLVTU  . The code will come to a blank screen, but the menu will appear by pressing  A . You will be presented with the following options:

SEQ : Animation sequence.

COL : Palette set.

TBL : Graphics set. Changing this will load the corresponding set of tiles into VRAM. You'll need to change this to view most sprites other than Mega Man.

TMR : Animation timer length. When this reaches zero, the next frame in the animation sequence is displayed.

[blank] : Current animation frame.

PLAYER 0/PLAYER 1/PLAYER 2 : Changed by pressing  Start . Seems to switch between three different banks of sprite animations.

SOUND : Sound test! Both music and sound effects can be played here.

The controls are as follows:

Up : Move cursor up.

Down : Move cursor down.

A : Select an option.

Select : Exits the sprite viewer (don't do this).

Start : Toggle between "Player 0", "Player 1", and "Player 2".

With an option selected:

Up : Increase ones digit by 1.

Down : Decrease ones digit by 1.

A : Increase tens digit by 1.

B : Exit option.

(Source: Rachel Mae)

Unused Graphics

Centaur Man

This sprite was supposed to be used when Centaur Man shoots his buster, lining up with one of his walking sprites. His shooting animation ends up using the other leg sprites instead, rendering this tile unused.

Flame Man

This sprite was supposed to be used when Flame Man uses Flame Blast, however they ended up using the same ones for both frames of the animation, resulting in Flame Man's knee being unnecessarily cut short.

Knight Man

This sprite was supposed to be used while Knight Man was airborne. Given how his grounded throwing animation is animated, it's possible this was supposed to be used for an aerial throwing animation.

Yamato Man

These sprites are supposed to be used when Yamato Man is standing still and ducking whilst his spear is without its tip. In-game, however, he starts moving as soon as he throws the spear tip, and only stops after recovering it. The player can force him to stop temporarily with Power Mega Man's charged attack, but the tip will be present on his spear.

Mr. X Castle / Wily Castle

Located within the Mr. X graphics are these weird spike sprites. Due to their design and placement immediately after the graphics for Mr. X's chair, it appears they were intended to be placed on the underside of his chair.

Enemies

Found within the graphics for the Count Bomb are these unused sprites for its plunger.

Found in the Metall's graphics are sprites for a right-facing helmet, and sprites for a spinning animation a la the Metall EXs from

Mega Man 4  . The former was likely to give the Metalls more detail by having the shine of their helmets facing the same direction. The latter were either intended for use in this game, given the very minor update they were given, or were leftovers from porting over their graphics from Mega Man 4.

Miscellaneous

These graphics are used to mark the different enemy and boss tile banks. "Zako", in this context, translates as "Small Fry", a common name used for small enemies and minions.

These tiles, on the other hand, mark different background areas. Since most background tile banks are completely filled, this doesn't pop up as much.

Some text saying DEMO - 1. Its purpose is unknown.

This question mark is stored in the same bank as the stage select graphics.  As seen in a pre-release screenshot , it was used as a placeholder graphic during development on the stage selection's Robot Masters portraits, as was the case with

Mega Man 5  .

Regional Differences

Audio Differences

Japan

US

The prologue music was redone for the American release due to the localized cutscene for the prologue being longer than the Japanese release's; the version of the game included in

Mega Man Anniversary Collection  , due to being based on the Japanese  Rockman Complete Works , uses the Japanese theme, resulting in the final parts of the cutscene playing out in silence.

Visual Differences

US

China

All instances of the name "MEGAMAN" in the North American version were changed into "ROCKMAN" for the Chinese version. This change also applies to the cutscene after defeating the final boss.

Japan

US

China

As was typical of the time, the North American title screen ditches the episodic subtitle (translated as "The Greatest Battle Ever!!").  In addition, all graphics and text were moved down to make room for the larger title and "Game Start" was changed to "Press Start".

Mega Man Anniversary Collection

reverts the text to "Game Start". As with the prior three games, the Chinese version takes the North American logo and makes it read "ROCK MAN".

The Japanese version features a nice bleed effect for the game title's appearance, which was probably removed from the American version because of the larger title.

Japan

US / China

On the stage select screen, "Push Start" was changed to "Press Start". Again, this was reverted in  Anniversary Collection .

Japan

US / China

In Rush Armor cutscene, all instances of "ROCKMAN" were replaced with "MEGAMAN", and the "6" was replaced with a roman numeral "VI".

Frustratingly, the Chinese version does NOT revert the names on this screen back to "ROCKMAN". And it was so close, too!

Japan

US / China

The letter tile for J used in the Weapon Select screen looks slightly different between the US and Japanese releases. Additionally, the Japanese version lacked letter tiles for D, O, Q, and Z, which were added in the US release of the game but go unused.

Revisional Differences

Mega Man Anniversary Collection

Title Screen (MMAC)

Japanese (Famicom)

International (NES)

Anniversary Collection

Like the other games in this collection, the title screen is based on the Japanese  Complete Works  version. As such:

To mimic the US version more, the logo doesn't "beam in" and it jumps straight to the planet fading into view.

The US  Mega Man  logo was used and stretched out.

Bizarrely, the "Mega Man" part was copied from the  first

three

games , as noted by the darker colors.

The "VI" of the US logo was slightly redesigned and lost its shadow.

The copyright information was updated and shifted.

Weirdly, the copyright symbol used is  also  from the the first three games, much like the "Mega Man" logo.

For some reason, the "registered trademark" emblem was replaced by general "trademark" text.

Other Changes

As the  Anniversary Collection  version of the game is based on the Japan-only  Complete Works  port, the game keeps the original prologue music but still includes the US version's dialogue for the cutscene. As such, there is an awkward silence when the music stops during the cutscene.

Original

Anniversary Collection

My scheme for world domination has faild!

My scheme for world domination has failed!

The typo on the word "failed" in Mr. X's English dialogue upon being defeated was corrected in  Anniversary Collection . Subsequent re-releases of the game have not retained this correction.
