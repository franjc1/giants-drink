F-Zero

&#91; a &#93;

is a 1990  racing game  developed and published by  Nintendo  for the  Super Nintendo Entertainment System  (SNES). It was released in Japan on November 21, 1990, in North America in August 1991, and in Europe in 1992.  F-Zero  is the first game in the

F-Zero  series  and was a  launch game  for the SNES.  F-Zero  was rereleased for the  Virtual Console  service on various Nintendo platforms and as part of the  Super NES Classic Edition  in 2017.

F-Zero  takes place in 2560, when multi-billionaires have created a new entertainment based on  Formula One  races called "F-Zero". The player can choose between one of four characters, each with their own hovercar. The player can race against computer-controlled characters in 15 tracks divided into three leagues

F-Zero  has been acknowledged by critics for setting the standard for the racing genre and the creation of its futuristic  subgenre . Critics lauded  F-Zero  for its fast and challenging gameplay, variety of tracks, and extensive use of the  Mode 7  graphics mode. This graphics-rendering technique was an innovative technological achievement at the time that made racing games more realistic, the first of which was  F-Zero . It is credited for reinvigorating the genre and inspiring the future creation of numerous racing games.

Gameplay

[

edit

]

Mode 7  allows the track to be scaled and rotated around the vehicle to simulate a 3D environment.

&#91; 1 &#93;

&#91; 2 &#93;

F-Zero  is a futuristic  racing game  in which players compete in a high-speed racing tournament called "F-Zero". There are four  F-Zero  characters. Each has their own selectable  hovercar , and each hovercar has its own unique performance abilities.

&#91; 3 &#93;

The objective is to beat opponents to the finish line while avoiding hazards such as slip zones and magnets that pull the vehicle off-center in an effort to make the player damage their vehicle or fall completely off the track. Each machine has a power meter, which serves as a measurement of the machine's durability; it decreases when the machine collides with land mines, the side of the track or another vehicle.

&#91; 4 &#93;

Energy can be replenished by driving over pit areas placed along the  home straight  or nearby.

&#91; 5 &#93;

A race in  F-Zero  consists of five laps around the track. The player must complete each lap in a successively higher place to avoid disqualification from the race. For each lap completed, the player is rewarded with an approximate four-second speed boost called the "Super Jet" and a number of points determined by place. An on-screen display will be shaded green to indicate that a boost can be used; however, the player is limited to saving up to three at a time. If a certain number of points are accumulated, an extra " spare machine " is acquired, which gives the player another chance to retry the course.

&#91; 4 &#93;

Tracks may feature two methods for temporarily boosting speeds; jump plates launch vehicles into the air thus providing additional acceleration for those not at full speed and dash zones greatly increases the racer's speed on the ground.

&#91; 5 &#93;

F-Zero  includes two modes of play. In the Grand Prix mode, the player chooses a league and races against other vehicles through each track in that league while avoiding disqualification. The Practice mode allows the player to practice seven of the courses from the Grand Prix mode.

&#91; 4 &#93;

F-Zero  has a total of fifteen tracks divided into three leagues ordered by increasing difficulty: Knight, Queen, and King. Furthermore, each league has four selectable  difficulty levels : beginner, standard, expert,

&#91; 4 &#93;

and master.

&#91; 6 &#93;

The multiple courses of Death Wind, Port Town, and Red Canyon have a pathway that is not accessible unless the player is on another iteration of those tracks, which then in turn closes the path previously available. Unlike most  F-Zero  games, there are three iterations of Mute City that show it in either a day, evening, or night setting with slightly different configurations. In  BS F-Zero 2 , Mute City IV continued the theme with an early morning setting. &#91;

citation needed

&#93;

Setting

[

edit

]

F-Zero  is set in the year 2560, when humanity's multiple encounters with alien life forms had resulted in the expansion of  Earth's  social framework. This led to commercial, technological and cultural interchanges between planets. The multi-billionaires who earned their wealth through  intergalactic  trade were mainly satisfied with their lifestyles, although most coveted more entertainment in their lives. This resulted in a new entertainment based on the  Formula One  races to be founded with vehicles that could hover one foot above the track. These Grand Prix races were soon named "F-Zero" after a rise in popularity of the races.

&#91; 3 &#93;

&#91; 4 &#93;

The game introduced the first set of  F-Zero  racers:  Captain Falcon , Dr. Stewart, Pico, and Samurai Goroh.

&#91; 3 &#93;

IGN  claimed Captain Falcon "was thrust into the limelight" in this game since he was the "star character".

&#91; 7 &#93;

An eight-page comic was included in its SNES manual that carried the reader through one of Captain Falcon's bounty missions.

&#91; 8 &#93;

Development

[

edit

]

F-Zero  was  produced  by  Shigeru Miyamoto  and directed by Kazunobu Shimizu, who also worked on art.

&#91; 9 &#93;

Takaya Imamura , one of the art designers, was surprised to be able to so freely design the characters and courses as he wanted since it was his first game.

&#91; 10 &#93;

Yasunari Nishida served as the main programmer. Nine people including three programmers worked  in house  on  F-Zero . It was common practice for personnel to take on multiple roles for SNES game development.

&#91; 9 &#93;

F-Zero  uses  Mode 7  is a form of  texture mapping  that allows a  raster graphical

plane  to be rotated and scaled freely, simulating the appearance of  3D  environments

&#91; 1 &#93;

without processing any polygons.

&#91; 3 &#93;

The Mode 7 rendering applied in  F-Zero  consists of a single-layer which is scaled and rotated around the vehicle.

&#91; 11 &#93;

This  pseudo-3D  capability of the SNES was designed to be represented by the game.

&#91; 12 &#93;

1UP.com 's Jeremy Parish stated that  F-Zero  and  Pilotwings  "existed almost entirely for the sake of showing [the system's pseudo-3D capabilities] off" as they outclassed the competition.

&#91; 13 &#93;

Release

[

edit

]

F-Zero  was released as a launch game for the SNES in Japan on November 21, 1990,

&#91; 14 &#93;

in North America in August 1991,

&#91; b &#93;

and in Europe in 1992.

&#91; 21 &#93;

&#91; 14 &#93;

&#91; 13 &#93;

F-Zero  was downloadable over the  Nintendo Power  peripheral in Japan

&#91; 22 &#93;

and was released as a demo on the  Nintendo Super System  in 1991.

&#91; 23 &#93;

&#91; 24 &#93;

An  F-Zero  jazz album was released on March 25, 1992, in Japan by  Tokuma Japan Communications .

&#91; 25 &#93;

&#91; 26 &#93;

It features twelve tracks on a single disc composed by Yumiko Kanki and Naoto Ishida, and arranged by Robert Hill and Michiko Hill. It also features Marc Russo (saxophones) of the  Yellowjackets  and  Robben Ford  (electric guitar).

&#91; 25 &#93;

F-Zero  was rereleased for the  Virtual Console  service on the  Wii  in late 2006,

&#91; 27 &#93;

on the  Wii U  in February 2013,

&#91; c &#93;

and the  New Nintendo 3DS  in March 2016.

&#91; 29 &#93;

Nintendo rereleased  F-Zero  in September 2017 as part of the  Super NES Classic Edition .

&#91; 30 &#93;

It was also included as one of the initial 20 SNES games for the  Nintendo Classics  service in September 2019.

Legacy

[

edit

]

See also:  List of  F-Zero  media

F-Zero  is credited for setting a standard for the racing genre

&#91; 36 &#93;

&#91; 55 &#93;

and inventing the "futuristic racing" subgenre.

&#91; 52 &#93;

&#91; 56 &#93;

&#91; 57 &#93;

IGN  credits it for having inspired the future creation of numerous racing games inside and out of the futuristic subgenre, including the

Wipeout  series  and

Daytona USA  .

&#91; 3 &#93;

&#91; 58 &#93;

Toshihiro Nagoshi , President of  Sega 's  Amusement Vision , stated in 2002 that  F-Zero  "actually taught me what a game should be" and that it served as an influence for him to create

Daytona USA

and other racing games.

&#91; 10 &#93;

Amusement Vision collaborated with Nintendo to develop

F-Zero GX

and

AX  , with Nagoshi serving as one of the co-producers for these games.

&#91; 10 &#93;

&#91; 59 &#93;

F-Zero  has continued to be included in lists of the best SNES games. In 1995,  Total!  rated it 50th on their Top 100 SNES Games summarizing: "It's old and basic but this garish futuristic offering still pushes your driving skills to the limit.

&#91; 60 &#93;

In 1997  Electronic Gaming Monthly  ranked it the 18th best console video game of all time, citing its tight controls, the different handling characteristics of the four craft, and the competitive opponent AI.

&#91; 61 &#93;

IGN  ranked  F-Zero  as the 91st best game ever in 2003, discussing its originality at time of release and as the 97th best game ever in 2005, describing it as still "respected as one of the all-time top racers".

&#91; 56 &#93;

&#91; 58 &#93;

ScrewAttack

placed it as the  18th best SNES game .

&#91; 62 &#93;

In 2018,

Complex

listed F-Zero 31st on its "The Best Super Nintendo Games of All Time."

&#91; 63 &#93;

Nintendo initially developed an  F-Zero  sequel for the SNES, although it was broadcast in several versions on the  St.GIGA  subscription service for the  Satellaview  attachment of the Super Famicom instead.

&#91; 11 &#93;

&#91; 38 &#93;

Using this add-on, players could download games via satellite and save it onto a flash  ROM  cartridge for temporary play.

&#91; 64 &#93;

The sequel was released under the Japanese names of  BS F-Zero Grand Prix  and  BS F-Zero Grand Prix 2  during the mid-1990s.

&#91; d &#93;

BS F-Zero Grand Prix  contained a new track and four different playable vehicles. According to

Nintendo Power  , the game was under consideration for a North American release via  Game Pak .

&#91; 67 &#93;

BS F-Zero Grand Prix 2  features one new league containing five tracks, a Grand Prix and a Practice mode.

&#91; 65 &#93;

The 1992 SNES game

Super Mario Kart

began as a multiplayer  F-Zero  prototype.

&#91; 68 &#93;

F-Zero  made the transition to 3D graphics with the release of

F-Zero X

on the  Nintendo 64  in 1998. Mode 7 effects continued to be used for the  Game Boy Advance  (GBA) games

Maximum Velocity

(2001),

&#91; 46 &#93;

GP Legend

(2003),

&#91; 69 &#93;

and

Climax

(2004).

&#91; 70 &#93;

&#91; 71 &#93;

Maximum Velocity  was described by  GameSpy  as a hard overhaul of  F-Zero  and featured improvements to its graphical effects.

&#91; 51 &#93;

&#91; 72 &#93;

Climax  features expanded backgrounds, improved track detail, and a more distinguishable separation between the course and ground below.

&#91; 70 &#93;

In 2023, Nintendo released a free  online  game,

F-Zero 99

(2023) through the  Nintendo Switch Online  (NSO) subscription service.

&#91; 73 &#93;

In a similar vein to the NSO exclusives like

Tetris 99

and

Pac-Man 99  , the game reuses graphical aesthetics and gameplay from the original  F-Zero , and recontextualizes it as a  battle royale  — players choose the same vehicles and tracks from the 1990 game.

&#91; 74 &#93;
