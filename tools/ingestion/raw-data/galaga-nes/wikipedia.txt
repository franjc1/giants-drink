Namco Galaga

&#91; 11 &#93;

Galaga

&#91; a &#93;

is a 1981  fixed shooter

video game  developed and published by  Namco  for Japanese and European  arcades ; it was distributed by  Midway Manufacturing  in North America. It is the sequel to 1979's

Galaxian  , and the second game in the

Galaxian  series . Controlling a starship, the player is tasked with destroying the Galaga forces in each stage while avoiding enemies and projectiles. Some enemies can capture a player's ship via a  tractor beam , which can be rescued by another ship to give the player a "dual fighter" with additional firepower.

Shigeru Yokoyama led development with a small team. Initial planning took about two months to finish. Originally developed for the  Namco Galaxian  arcade board, it was instead shifted to a new system as suggested by Namco's Research and Development division. Inspiration for the dual fighter mechanic was taken from a film that Yokoyama had seen prior to development, where a ship was captured using a large circular beam. The project became immensely popular around the company, with Namco's president  Masaya Nakamura  even taking interest.

Although early location tests were unsuccessful,  Galaga  went on to become one of the most successful titles of the  golden age of arcade video games , routinely appearing on Japanese and American arcade charts through 1987. It was acclaimed by critics for its gameplay, innovation, addictive nature and improvements made over its predecessor, and is widely regarded as one of the  greatest video games of all time . Several home ports were released for a multitude of platforms, including the  MSX ,  Atari 7800 , and  NES , alongside releases on digital distribution platforms such as  Xbox Live Arcade  and appearances in many  Namco compilations . It was followed by

Gaplus

in 1984.

Gameplay

[

edit

]

Gameplay with a Boss Galaga using a tractor beam, while the player exchanges shots with aliens in formation in stage 4.

Galaga  is a fixed shooter. The player controls a lone starfighter at the bottom of the screen, which must prevent the Galaga forces from destroying all of mankind.

&#91; 12 &#93;

The objective of each stage is to defeat all of the Galaga aliens, which will fly into formation from the top and sides of the screen.

&#91; 13 &#93;

Similar to  Galaxian , aliens will dive towards the player while shooting down projectiles; colliding with either projectiles or aliens will result in a life being lost.

&#91; 13 &#93;

Atop the enemy formation are four large aliens known as the "Boss Galaga", which take two shots to destroy.

&#91; 13 &#93;

These aliens can use a  tractor beam  to capture the player's ship, returning with it to the top of the formation and costing the player a life.

&#91; 13 &#93;

Should additional lives remain, the player has an opportunity to shoot down the Boss Galaga holding the captured ship. Shooting it down as it dives towards the player will result in the captured ship being rescued, and it will join the player's ship, transforming it into a "dual-fighter" with additional firepower and a larger  hitbox .

&#91; 13 &#93;

However, destroying a Boss Galaga with a captured ship while it is in formation will instead cause the fighter to turn against the player and act as an alien.

&#91; 13 &#93;

The ship will return in a later level as part of the formation.

Some enemies can morph into new enemy types with different attack patterns, including the Galaxian Flagship.

&#91; 13 &#93;

Stages are indicated by emblems at the bottom-right of the screen.

&#91; 13 &#93;

Enemies become more aggressive as the game progresses, increasing their number of projectiles and diving down at a faster rate.

&#91; 12 &#93;

The third stage and every fourth thereafter is a bonus stage, where the aliens fly in a preset formation without firing at the player.

&#91; 13 &#93;

Development

[

edit

]

Galaga  was created by Japanese developer Shigeru Yokoyama, a long-time veteran of  Namco .

&#91; 15 &#93;

Namco's first big video game hit in arcades was

Galaxian

(1979);

&#91; 16 &#93;

&#91; 17 &#93;

the game's success led Namco to produce a large number of  Namco Galaxian  arcade boards to keep up with demand.

&#91; 15 &#93;

By the early 1980s the game was becoming harder to sell, so to help clear out inventory, Yokoyama was tasked with creating two new games that could run on the Namco Galaxian board.

&#91; 15 &#93;

The first of these was

King & Balloon

(1980),

&#91; 15 &#93;

a fixed shooter that is cited as the first video game to incorporate speech.

&#91; 18 &#93;

The second game was instead made for newer hardware as suggested by Namco's Research & Development division.

&#91; 15 &#93;

This new arcade board was named the  Namco Galaga  and was used in games including

Bosconian

(1981)

&#91; 19 &#93;

and

Dig Dug

(1982).

&#91; 20 &#93;

Although Yokoyama was not given explicit instructions to make a shooting game, management expressed desire for him to make a game similar to  Galaxian .

&#91; 15 &#93;

Initial planning for the project took two months.

&#91; 15 &#93;

The idea for the dual fighter stemmed from Yokoyama wanting to create enemies with different attack styles.

&#91; 15 &#93;

The tractor beam emitted by the Boss Galaga was inspired by a film in which a character's ship was captured by a circling laser.

&#91; 15 &#93;

Yokoyama incorporated this idea into  Galaga , whereby an enemy could capture the player's ship with a beam and the ship would need to be rescued.

&#91; 15 &#93;

Originally, rescuing a captured ship would award the player an extra life, but this was soon changed to having it fight alongside the player.

&#91; 15 &#93;

This idea proved to be a problem at first; due to hardware limitations, the game could only display a limited number of  sprites , resulting in the dual-fighter being unable to shoot any more missiles.

&#91; 15 &#93;

As a workaround, Yokoyama made a 16x16 sprite for the ship and a 16x16 sprite for the bullets, reducing the total sprite count by two.

&#91; 15 &#93;

Inspired by the intermissions in

Pac-Man

(1980) and bonus stages in

Rally-X

(1980), Yokoyama added a special bonus level.

&#91; 15 &#93;

While planning, lead programmer Tetsu Ogawa informed him of a  bug  whereby enemies would simply fly off the screen instead of moving into formation.

&#91; 15 &#93;

Ogawa expressed interest in incorporating the idea into the game, leading to the inclusion of the Challenging Stages.

&#91; 15 &#93;

Enemies originally flew in one type of pattern, with more being added to increase replay value.

&#91; 15 &#93;

Graphic designer  Hiroshi Ono  designed many of the sprites, including the player's ship and the Boss Galaga alien.

&#91; 15 &#93;

Prior to location testing, the team focused on designing the instruction card, a sheet of paper that showed how the game was played.

&#91; 15 &#93;

The text was done by the planners, while the actual design was handled by a graphic artist.

&#91; 15 &#93;

The card originally showed the control layout and the basics of the game, which was stripped early on for being too boring.

&#91; 15 &#93;

Yokoyama suggested that the card instead show off the dual fighter mechanic, as a means to draw in players.

&#91; 15 &#93;

The team kept bringing in designs to Namco president  Masaya Nakamura , who continued to reject them until he ordered the team to simply make it in front of him.

&#91; 15 &#93;

The team was allowed to set their own deadlines, due to Namco's then-laidback corporate structure.

&#91; 15 &#93;

Feedback on the project was given by Nakamura and other employees, including  Pac-Man  creator  Toru Iwatani .

&#91; 15 &#93;

Despite the game's immense popularity around the company, early location tests failed to meet expectations due to players being able to progress a long way with only one coin, thus generating low income.

&#91; 15 &#93;

Although Yokoyama stated that the game's popularity could still generate income, Namco executives instructed the team to increase the difficulty level.

&#91; 15 &#93;

Galaga  was released in Japan in September 1981;

&#91; 21 &#93;

Midway Manufacturing , who had previously licensed  Galaxian , released the game in North America in November of that year.

&#91; 1 &#93;

Ports and re-releases

[

edit

]

Sega-Galaga,  the first home conversion of  Galaga,  was developed and published by  Sega  for the  SG-1000  in 1983.

&#91; 22 &#93;

An MSX version followed in 1984.

&#91; 23 &#93;

A conversion for the  Family Computer  was released in 1985 in Japan,

&#91; 24 &#93;

which was later released internationally by  Bandai  for the  Nintendo Entertainment System , subtitled  Demons of Death  in North America.

&#91; 13 &#93;

Atari Corporation  released a version for the  Atari 7800  in 1986 as one of the console's thirteen launch games.

&#91; 25 &#93;

In Europe,  Aardvark Software  released an unofficial port for the  BBC Micro  and  Acorn Electron  called  Zalaga  in 1983 which was described by

Computer and Video Games

as "true to the arcade original".

&#91; 26 &#93;

Namco published a  Game Boy  version in Japan in 1995, which was bundled with  Galaxian .

&#91; 27 &#93;

Nintendo  published the game outside Japan under the

Arcade Classic

brand.

&#91; 28 &#93;

Two mobile phone versions were released, both confined to Japan; the first was for  i-Mode  in 2001,

&#91; 29 &#93;

and the second for  EZweb  in 2006.

&#91; 30 &#93;

The original arcade version was released for the  Xbox Live Arcade  service in 2006, featuring online leaderboards and achievements.

&#91; 31 &#93;

The NES release was ported to the  Wii Virtual Console  in 2007,

&#91; 32 &#93;

followed by the arcade version in 2009.

&#91; 33 &#93;

A  Roku  port was published in 2011.

&#91; 34 &#93;

In 2013, the NES version was released on both the  3DS  and  Wii U Virtual Console .

&#91; 32 &#93;

Galaga  was one of the four games released under the

Arcade Game Series

brand, which was published for the  Xbox One ,  PlayStation 4 , and  Microsoft Windows  in 2016.

&#91; 35 &#93;

Galaga  was included in Namco compilations including

Namco Museum Vol. 1

(1995),

&#91; 36 &#93;

Namco Museum 64

(1996),

&#91; 37 &#93;

Namco Museum 50th Anniversary

(2005),

&#91; 38 &#93;

Namco Museum Virtual Arcade

(2008),

&#91; 39 &#93;

Namco Museum Essentials

(2009),

&#91; 40 &#93;

and

Namco Museum Megamix

(2010).

&#91; 41 &#93;

The 2010  Wii  game

Pac-Man Party

and its 2011 Nintendo 3DS version include  Galaga  as an extra, alongside the arcade versions of

Dig Dug

and

Pac-Man  .

&#91; 42 &#93;

&#91; 43 &#93;

In celebration of the game's 30th anniversary in 2011, a high-definition remake was released for  iOS  devices as part of

Galaga 30th Collection  , which also included remakes of

Galaxian  ,

Gaplus

and

Galaga '88  .

&#91; 44 &#93;

Alongside the  Xbox 360  and  PlayStation 3  sequel

Galaga Legions  , it was ported to the  Nintendo 3DS  in 2011 as part of

Pac-Man & Galaga Dimensions  .

&#91; 45 &#93;

The original version was also added to the iOS

Namco Arcade

compilation in 2012.

&#91; 46 &#93;

The NES release is one of 30 games included in the  NES Classic Edition .

&#91; 47 &#93;

Galaga  was met with acclaim, with many applauding the addictive nature, gameplay structure, innovation and improvements over its predecessor, and was a popular game during the  golden age of arcade video games .

&#91; 57 &#93;

In Japan, it was the sixth highest-grossing arcade game of 1981,

&#91; 59 &#93;

then the third highest-grossing arcade game of 1982,

&#91; 60 &#93;

and then the ninth highest-grossing  table arcade cabinet  of 1986.

&#91; 61 &#93;

&#91; 62 &#93;

In North America, it regularly appeared on the monthly sales chart of  RePlay  magazine from April 1980 to April 1987, being only outdone by  Sega 's

Monaco GP

(1979).

&#91; 63 &#93;

Galaga  was among the top ten highest-grossing arcade games of 1983 in the United States.

&#91; 64 &#93;

&#91; 65 &#93;

Computer + Video Games

praised the challenge and improvements made over

Galaxian  ,

&#91; 52 &#93;

while  Arcade Express  selected it as an "Honorable Mention" in their 1983 arcade awards.

&#91; 66 &#93;

Japanese publication  Amusement Life  said that the sense of thrill and fast-paced action made  Galaga  a "must play", while also praising its unique dual-fighter mechanic and colorful graphics.

&#91; 67 &#93;

Vidiot  magazine listed it as the seventh best arcade game of 1983, saying that its gameplay variety, dual-fighter mechanic and bonus stages made it stick out from the crowd.

&#91; 68 &#93;

In a 1998 retrospective review,

AllGame

said the strategy stood out amongst other games of its type, describing the gameplay as "perfectly balanced shooting action."

&#91; 48 &#93;

Reviewing the NES home version,

GameSpy

called  Galaga  a "must play for arcade freaks", praising the port's accurate representation of the arcade version in terms of its graphics, sound effects and gameplay.

&#91; 56 &#93;

IGN

also praised the NES port's element of strategy within the dual-fighter mechanic and addictive gameplay.

&#91; 57 &#93;

GameSpot  , in their review for the Xbox 360 release, stated the gameplay was "as tricky as it ever was", praising the inclusion of online leaderboards and for being a faithful arcade conversion.

&#91; 55 &#93;

Eurogamer

agreed, citing that the leaderboards add to addictiveness.

&#91; 53 &#93;

Ports of  Galaga  received praise for their faithfulness to the arcade original.

Nintendo Life

praised the  3DS Virtual Console  port of the NES version for remaining accurate to the original, stating that it "aged surprisingly well" and was worth revisiting.

&#91; 32 &#93;

Games  magazine  praised the improvements over games such as

Space Invaders

and

Galaxian  , commenting that  Galaga  still holds up years later.

&#91; 69 &#93;

Joystick  magazine praised the NES version's accurate portrayal of the arcade original, notably in its graphics and gameplay structure.

&#91; 58 &#93;

Famicom Tsūshin

commended the Game Boy version's faithful conversion alongside its support for the  Super Game Boy  peripheral,

&#91; 27 &#93;

while  Electric Playground  stated that it should " be near the top of your Game Boy's next purchase list".

&#91; 28 &#93;

Some publications expressed disappointment towards home releases for lacking extra features.

GameSpot

disliked the lack of online multiplayer in the Xbox 360 release, as well as the lack of an updated graphics setting, saying that the port was "awfully bare bones" compared to other XBLA releases.  Eurogamer  expressed distaste towards the Xbox 360 port's high price point, as well as the achievements for being "insultingly easy" to obtain.

&#91; 53 &#93;

Eurogamer  also agreed with  GameSpot  in the lack of online multiplayer.

&#91; 53 &#93;

Galaga  has been listed by numerous publications among the  greatest video games of all time .  Flux  magazine ranked it at #57 on their "Top 100 Video Games" in 1995,

&#91; 70 &#93;

while

Game Informer

listed it at #23 in their "Top 200 Games of All Time" in 2010.

Next Generation

ranked it at #96 in their "Top 100 Games of All Time" for its innovation to shoot 'em up games as a whole,

&#91; 71 &#93;

and at #17 in their "Top 50 Games of All Time".

&#91; 72 &#93;

Game Informer

labeled it the 19th greatest video game ever made in 2001, calling it the best game of the fixed-shooter genre.

&#91; 73 &#93;

Electronic Gaming Monthly

listed it at #20 in their "100 Best Games of All Time" in 1997

&#91; 74 &#93;

and "Top 100 Games of All Time" in 2001,

&#91; 75 &#93;

and as #28 in their "Greatest 200 Videogames of Their Time" in 2006.

&#91; 76 &#93;

GameFAQs  users voted it the 15th greatest game ever made in 2004

&#91; 77 &#93;

and the 10th in 2009.

&#91; 78 &#93;

GameSpy  staff voted it the eighth best arcade game of all time in 2011.

&#91; 79 &#93;

It was ranked at #93 in  IGN 's "Top 100 Games of All Time" for its addictive gameplay and long-standing appeal.

&#91; 80 &#93;

The  Killer List of Videogames  listed it as #27 in their "Top 100 Video Games" list, as well as the 4th most collected arcade game and 2nd most popular on their website.

&#91; 81 &#93;

Electronic Gaming Monthly

listed  Ms. Pac-Man/Galaga - Class of 1981  as the second best arcade game of all time for its inclusion of both games.

&#91; 82 &#93;

Ms. Pac-Man

and titled  Ms. Pac-Man / Galaga - Class of 1981 .

&#91; 83 &#93;

&#91; 85 &#93;

&#91; 86 &#93;

&#91; 87 &#93;

(1983),
