Metal Combat: Falcon's Revenge

is a 1993  light gun shooter

video game  developed by  Intelligent Systems  and published by  Nintendo  for the  Super Nintendo Entertainment System . It is the sequel to

Battle Clash

(1992) and one of several titles that require the  Super Scope

light gun . Taking place three years after the events of its predecessor, the player acts as the gunner of the Standing Tank (ST) Falcon piloted by Mike Anderson, fighting a group of chiefs in the Battle Game, the returning emperor Anubis, and the invading Eltorian alien race.

Metal Combat  was created by Team Battle Clash, a group within Intelligent Systems made up of  Nintendo R&D1  staff, which previously worked on  Battle Clash . It was directed by chief graphic designer Toshitaka Muramatsu, who worked in the

Fire Emblem

and

Paper Mario

series, and produced by  Gunpei Yokoi . The music was composed by  Yuka Tsujiyoko , who also scored  Battle Clash . The game garnered generally favorable reception from critics; praise was given to the refined gameplay, different strategies of each enemy, variety of modes, and overall improvements made over its predecessor. Some reviewers criticized the single-player campaign for its short duration, repetitive action, and difficulty.

Gameplay

[

edit

]

Internal view of the ST Falcon, battling against the ST Cobra on Saturn

See also:  Battle Clash §&#160;Gameplay

Like its predecessor,  Metal Combat: Falcon's Revenge  is a  light-gun shooter  game which requires the  Super Scope

light gun  peripheral for the  Super Nintendo Entertainment System  to be played.

&#91; 1 &#93;

&#91; 2 &#93;

&#91; 3 &#93;

The plot takes place three years after the events occurred in

Battle Clash  ; the emperor Anubis suffered defeat at the hands of Mike Anderson and a new age of prosperity began to emerge on Earth. However, Anubis returned to resume his tyranny and an alien race called the Eltorians entered the  Solar System  to conquer Earth.

&#91; 3 &#93;

&#91; 4 &#93;

&#91; 5 &#93;

Gameplay consists of four different modes divided into single-player or multiplayer submenus: Battle, Time Trial, Training, and Combat.

&#91; 6 &#93;

&#91; 7 &#93;

Battle is a single-player scenario where the player acts as the gunner of the Standing Tank (ST) Falcon piloted by Anderson, fighting a group of chiefs in the Battle Game, the returning emperor Anubis, and the invading Eltorian alien race in one-on-one duels.

&#91; 3 &#93;

&#91; 7 &#93;

&#91; 8 &#93;

Time Trial is a mode where the player must defeat enemies to achieve the best time possible.

&#91; 5 &#93;

&#91; 6 &#93;

&#91; 7 &#93;

Training is a tutorial mode, in which an instructor named Rola helps beginners through a series of seven lessons.

&#91; 2 &#93;

&#91; 3 &#93;

&#91; 7 &#93;

Combat is a versus mode, where one player controls the ST Falcon using the Super Scope while the other player controls one of the enemy STs using a Super NES controller.

&#91; 1 &#93;

&#91; 3 &#93;

&#91; 4 &#93;

The player attacks enemies using rapid fire by holding down the fire button, energy bolts, bombs, and an array of special  items .

&#91; 2 &#93;

&#91; 3 &#93;

&#91; 6 &#93;

The ST Falcon charges energy bolts up to three times when the player is not firing and fires a treble shot once the power meters are filled.

&#91; 3 &#93;

&#91; 4 &#93;

The player counters enemy fire by intercepting their projectiles, though some enemy attacks can only be deflected by shooting energy bolts.

&#91; 4 &#93;

&#91; 5 &#93;

Each enemy ST has destructible weak points that the player must shoot to deal damage.

&#91; 3 &#93;

&#91; 4 &#93;

&#91; 9 &#93;

Completing the single-player mode unlocks the ST Tornado, piloted by Carol Eugene.

&#91; 3 &#93;

&#91; 4 &#93;

&#91; 7 &#93;

The ST Tornado can store up to ten energy beams and fire each one individually but has less defense compared to the ST Falcon.

&#91; 3 &#93;

Development and release

[

edit

]

Metal Combat: Falcon's Revenge  was created by Team Battle Clash, a group within  Intelligent Systems  made up of  Nintendo R&D1  staff that developed games for the  Super Scope

light gun , which had previously worked on

Battle Clash

(1992).

&#91; 3 &#93;

&#91; 10 &#93;

&#91; 11 &#93;

The game is one of several titles that requires the Super Scope.

&#91; 12 &#93;

It was directed by chief graphic designer Toshitaka Muramatsu, who worked in the

Fire Emblem

and

Paper Mario

series, and produced by  Gunpei Yokoi .

&#91; 3 &#93;

&#91; 13 &#93;

&#91; 14 &#93;

&#91; 15 &#93;

&#91; 16 &#93;

Shinya Yamamoto and Toshihiro Nishii acted as two of the game's co-programmers, who later worked on

Tetris Attack  .

&#91; a &#93;

&#91; 13 &#93;

&#91; 14 &#93;

The music was scored by  Yuka Tsujiyoko , who also composed  Battle Clash .

&#91; 13 &#93;

&#91; 17 &#93;

Nintendo first published  Metal Combat: Falcon's Revenge  for the  Super Nintendo Entertainment System  in North America in December 1993, followed by Europe in May 1994.

&#91; 8 &#93;

&#91; 18 &#93;

It was originally titled  Battle Clash II , but the name was changed shortly before its North American launch.

&#91; 19 &#93;

The game was housed in a 16- megabit  (2  MB ) cartridge using the  OBC-1 , a sprite manipulation enhancement chip.

&#91; 8 &#93;

&#91; 20 &#93;

Metal Combat  was only released in Western regions, as the Super Scope proved to be less popular in Japan.

&#91; 3 &#93;

&#91; 4 &#93;

&#91; 10 &#93;

The cover was illustrated by artist Stephen Peringer.

&#91; 21 &#93;

Metal Combat: Falcon's Revenge  received generally favorable reception from critics and retrospective commentators.

&#91; 3 &#93;

&#91; 4 &#93;

&#91; 24 &#93;

&#91; 25 &#93;

&#91; 26 &#93;

Nintendo Power

considered it to be a much improved follow-up to

Battle Clash

and highlighted its multiplayer mode; however, they found the single-player campaign weak, pointing out the difficulty of seeing incoming enemy fire.

&#91; 9 &#93;

Electronic Gaming Monthly

' s  reviewers celebrated the game for having colorful graphics and deeper gameplay than most  Super Scope  titles, and cited the two-player mode as the best feature, although they did not like its difficulty.

&#91; 22 &#93;

Game Players

' s Chris Slate lauded each of the game's enemies for their different strategies, as well as the soundscapes, variety of modes, and replay value.

&#91; 1 &#93;

GamePro

' s Captain Squideo described it as "much harder, faster, and ultimately better than any previous Super Scope game, including  Battle Clash ". Squideo praised the detailed graphics that allow the player to spot enemy weak points, but criticized the text between fights and low-quality voice samples.

&#91; 2 &#93;

Hobby Consolas

'  Antonio Caravaca praised the game's visual quality, sound effects, difficulty, and two-player mode, but criticized the occasional flickering that occurs during explosions.

&#91; 6 &#93;

Nintendo Acción

gave favorable remarks to the game's mech designs, audio, and various game modes, but criticized the story mode for its short duration.

&#91; 7 &#93;

Player One  ' s Jean-Pierre Abidal found the action in the game to be fun but repetitive.

&#91; 23 &#93;

Hardcore Gaming 101

' s David DeRienzo lauded the game's improved audiovisual presentation, refined combat system, and modes of play, writing that " Metal Combat  manages to surpass its predecessor in every imaginable way while still offering the same great core play experience".

&#91; 3 &#93;

Destructoid

' s Zoey Handley regarded it as one of the best  light gun  games ever developed.

&#91; 4 &#93;
