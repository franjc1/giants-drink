Dragon Ball Z: Super Butōden  ,

&#91; b &#93;

known as

Dragon Ball Z

in Europe, is a 1993  fighting

video game  developed by  Tose  and published by  Bandai  for the  Super Nintendo Entertainment System . It is based upon  Akira Toriyama 's

Dragon Ball

franchise, and was its first fighting game.

Due to the popularity of

Street Fighter II: The World Warrior

at the time, producer Toshihiro Suzuki chose to work on a fighting game when assigned to a new project due to it being his preferred genre. Its gameplay consists of one-on-one fights, with a main six-button configuration, featuring special moves, as well as three playable modes.

Dragon Ball Z: Super Butōden  garnered mostly positive reception from critics; most reviewers praised the presentation and gameplay but others felt divided in regards to several design aspects. The game sold approximately 1.3 million units in its first two months of release in Japan.

&#91; 4 &#93;

Super Butōden  would spawn several sequels; including

Dragon Ball Z: Super Butōden 2

(1993),

Dragon Ball Z: Super Butōden 3

(1994),

Dragon Ball Z: Buyū Retsuden

(1994),

Dragon Ball Z: Shin Butōden

(1995),

Dragon Ball Kai: Ultimate Butōden

(2011), and

Dragon Ball Z: Extreme Butōden

(2015).

Gameplay

[

edit

]

A match between  Goku  and  Vegeta  on planet  Namek

Dragon Ball Z: Super Butōden  is a fighting game similar to  Street Fighter II . Players fight against other characters in one-on-one matches and the fighter who manages to deplete the health bar of the opponent wins the bout and becomes the winner of the match.

&#91; 5 &#93;

&#91; 6 &#93;

The game features three modes of play, a roster of 10 playable characters and their respective transformations with one additional transformation being unlockable via cheat code. Characters available are  Goku ,  Piccolo ,  Vegeta ,  Frieza ,  Android 20 ,  Android 18 ,  Android 16 ,  Cell ,  Trunks  and  Gohan .

&#91; 7 &#93;

Super Butōden  uses a customizable six-button control scheme.

&#91; 5 &#93;

&#91; 7 &#93;

Special moves are present in conventional format, with most commands consisting of button combinations.

&#91; 7 &#93;

Characters can also dash back and forth by pressing L and R respectively.

&#91; 5 &#93;

&#91; 6 &#93;

&#91; 7 &#93;

Unique to the game is the split-screen feature;

&#91; 8 &#93;

when one of the two combatants moves far away in the playfield, the view is split to keep both fighters shown in a single screen while their positioning is kept via an on-screen radar.

&#91; 5 &#93;

Development and release

[

edit

]

Dragon Ball Z: Super Butōden  served as both the first full-fledged fighting game in the  Dragon Ball  franchise and the first entry in the  Super Butōden  sub-series.

&#91; 9 &#93;

Its development was helmed by producer Toshihiro Suzuki, with Akihumi Kubota and Shinsaku Shimada serving as co-directors.

&#91; 10 &#93;

&#91; 11 &#93;

Kinya Takehana and Koji Shimizu co-designed the game with additional design work by Design Office D&D while artists Hirokazu Tamai, Masayuki Takahashi, Seiichiro Shiino, Shinichi Iidawere and Shōko Arai were responsible for the  pixel art , in addition to Maruko Papa and "Maruko" acting as programmers.

&#91; 10 &#93;

Both music and sound design were handled by  Kenji Yamamoto  of Hyper Entertainment and Yuki Sabakuma respectively.

&#91; 10 &#93;

&#91; 12 &#93;

Other people also assisted with its development.

&#91; 10 &#93;

&#91; 13 &#93;

Due to the popularity of fighting games at the time such as  Street Fighter II: The World Warrior , Suzuki stated he chose to work on a fighting game when assigned to a new project due to it being his favorite genre.

&#91; 11 &#93;

Dragon Ball Z: Super Butōden  was first released by Bandai in Japan on March 20, 1993, and later in Europe on November 30 of the same year.

&#91; 14 &#93;

&#91; 15 &#93;

On March 27, 1993, an album was published exclusively in Japan by  Columbia , featuring arranged songs co-composed by Yamamoto, Ginji Ogawa and Sueaki Harada of Hyper Monolith.

&#91; 12 &#93;

&#91; 16 &#93;

In Japan, a special glitter sticker with a hidden technique written on the back for the game was also distributed as an order bonus.

&#91; 17 &#93;

In 2018, the title was included as a limited pre-order bonus for the  Nintendo Switch  version of

Dragon Ball FighterZ  , marking its first appearance in North American regions.

&#91; 18 &#93;

&#91; 19 &#93;

&#91; 20 &#93;

On October of the same year,  Bandai Namco Entertainment  announced that the game would be unlocked for free to Switch users in Latin America if their official newsletter reached 10,000 subscribers.

&#91; 9 &#93;

Sales

[

edit

]

Upon release in Japan,  Dragon Ball Z: Super Butōden  topped the

Famitsu

sales charts in March 1993.

&#91; 36 &#93;

&#91; 37 &#93;

Two months after its release in Japan, the title recorded sales of 1.3 million units.

&#91; 38 &#93;

It proved to be a commercial hit, with lifetime sales between 1.41 million and, according to  Famitsu , 1.45 million units, in Japan alone.

&#91; 11 &#93;

&#91; 39 &#93;

Critical response

[

edit

]

Dragon Ball Z: Super Butōden  received mostly positive reception from critics.

&#91; 24 &#93;

&#91; 40 &#93;

Famitsu  praised the voice samples, the split-screen feature, ability to avoid special moves via button inputs and auto mode for newcomers.

&#91; 23 &#93;

Fan reception was positive: readers of

Famimaga

voted to give the game a 23.08 out of 30 score, ranking at the number 46 spot in a poll, indicating a large popular following.

&#91; 35 &#93;

Famimaga  also gave it several awards for character and originality respectively.

&#91; 35 &#93;

Spirit, Laurent Defrance and François Hermellin of

Consoles +

&#160;&#91; fr &#93;  praised the audiovisual presentation, playability and longevity.

&#91; 22 &#93;

Olivier Prézeau, Grégoire Hellot and Laurent Deheppe of

Joypad

&#160;&#91; fr &#93;  commended the digitized sounds, aerial combat, character roster and sprite animations but criticized various aspects such as the visuals for being poor and the French translation.

&#91; 26 &#93;

&#91; 27 &#93;

Super Pro  ' s Ryan Butt gave positive remarks to the graphics and sound, characters and their special moves, as well as regarding the split-screen effect to be novel but noted it could become cumbersome, while criticizing the gameplay for being repetitive.

&#91; 34 &#93;

Super Action  ' s Frank O'Connor gave a more negative outlook to the game, regarding it to be an "unplayable"  Street Fighter II  clone.

&#91; 32 &#93;

Super Play

' s  Matt Bielby  gave positive comment to the graphics, cast of characters, originality and number of special moves but noted it to be "hard to access."

&#91; 30 &#93;

Play Time

' s Klaus Martin also commended the audiovisual presentation but felt mixed in regards to the gameplay.

&#91; 31 &#93;

Super Control  ' s Allison West and Paul Mallinson also felt mixed about the visuals, sound, gameplay and overall longevity.

&#91; 33 &#93;

In contrast, Sylvain of

Player One

&#160;&#91; fr &#93;  gave a very positive review of the title, giving it high remarks in multiple areas.

&#91; 28 &#93;

Spanish magazine

Nintendo Acción

gave very high remarks to the visuals, audio, gameplay and fun factor.

&#91; 8 &#93;

Bruno Sol of

Superjuegos

&#160;&#91; es &#93;  praised its faithfulness to the original manga, graphics, sound design and number of moves for each character, regarding it as one of the best arcade-style fighting games for Super Nintendo.

&#91; 29 &#93;

Jeuxvideo.com

' s Rroyd-Y stated that "This first game in a successful trilogy is surely not the best episode of the three. Stained by a very heavy gameplay and a disconcerting difficulty, it will really only delight the unconditional fans of the time who seek at all costs to slip into the skin of Son Goku or Vegeta."

&#91; 25 &#93;
