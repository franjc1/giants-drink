Dungeon Keeper

is a  strategy video game  developed by  Bullfrog Productions  and released by  Electronic Arts  in June 1997 for  MS-DOS  and Windows 95. In  Dungeon Keeper , the player builds and manages a  dungeon , protecting it from invading 'hero' characters intent on stealing accumulated treasures, killing monsters and ultimately the player's demise. The ultimate goal is to conquer the world by destroying the heroic forces and rival dungeon keepers in each realm. A character known as the Avatar (resembling the  Avatar  from

Ultima VIII: Pagan  ) appears as the final hero.  Dungeon Keeper  uses  Creative Technology 's  SoundFont  technology to enhance its atmosphere. Multiplayer with up to four players is supported using a modem, or over a local network.

Dungeon Keeper  took over two years to develop, and an expansion pack, a  Direct3D  version, and a level editor were released. Midway through development, lead developer  Peter Molyneux  decided to leave Bullfrog when the game was complete, which was the motivation for its success. Versions for the  Sega Saturn  and  Sony PlayStation  were in development, but cancelled. The game received critical acclaim, with reviewers praising the uniqueness and depth.  Dungeon Keeper  was re-released on  GOG.com  in 2011,

Origin  in 2016, and  Steam  in 2024. A fan-made  mod ,  KeeperFX , was released, which fixes bugs and adds features.  Dungeon Keeper  was followed by a sequel,

Dungeon Keeper 2  , in 1999, and influenced games such as

Lego Rock Raiders

and

Ghost Master  .

Gameplay

[

edit

]

Navigating the built-up dungeon with the Hand of Evil

The player constructs and manages a dungeon, recruiting and catering for minions to run it and defend it from enemy invaders.

&#91; 1 &#93;

&#91; 2 &#93;

The primary method of control is the hand, used to pick up creatures and objects in the dungeon, carry them around, and drop them. The hand allows the player to 'slap' creatures and objects, and interact with them.

&#91; 3 &#93;

Dungeon Keeper  's  gameplay exemplifies a  dark sense of humour .

&#91; 4 &#93;

The Dungeon Heart represents the Keeper's link to the world. If it is destroyed, the player loses.

&#91; 5 &#93;

Along with the heart, the player begins with a small number of  imps , the generic work force for dungeon activities: they dig tunnels into the surrounding soil, capture enemy rooms and Portals, mine  gold  and  gems , and set traps. Imps are obtained by using the Create Imp spell.

&#91; 6 &#93;

Slapping creatures forces them to work faster temporarily, but removes some of their  health .

&#91; 7 &#93;

Gold is obtained primarily by digging Gold Seams, and Gem Seams provide an unlimited supply, though take longer to accumulate. Gold is used to build rooms, cast spells, and train creatures.

&#91; 1 &#93;

&#91; 8 &#93;

&#91; 9 &#93;

To order the imps to dig a tile, the player need flag the tile.

&#91; 10 &#93;

Throughout the game, a "mentor" will advise the player as to various happenings and problems within the dungeon, as a sinister voiceover.

&#91; 11 &#93;

Once the Imps are working, the player must then set up a basic  infrastructure : Lairs for monsters, a Hatchery (where chickens, which serve as food, are grown), and a Treasury. After connecting the dungeon to a 'Portal', minions will arrive. Minions include dragons, warlocks, and the horned reaper, as well as undead creatures such as vampires and skeletons.

&#91; 1 &#93;

As the game progresses, the player moves along a technology tree, unlocking further rooms and spells.

&#91; 8 &#93;

Rooms can only be built on tiles belonging to the player.

&#91; 10 &#93;

The player is red and the tiles are coloured accordingly. Other keepers have different colours, and the heroes are white. Unaligned creatures and rooms are multicoloured.

&#91; 12 &#93;

The player can build traps and doors, created in the workshop. Traps include lightning and boulder traps, the latter instantly killing creatures it comes into contact with. As with rooms, they can only be built on tiles that belong to the player. Traps are not built instantly; Imps need to place them on the blueprint.

&#91; 8 &#93;

&#91; 13 &#93;

The Temple is a room where creatures are made happy, and the player can sacrifice creatures to the dark gods. The gods may reward or punish the player, or be indifferent depending on the sacrifice.

&#91; 1 &#93;

&#91; 5 &#93;

The dungeon has a fleshed-out  ecology : certain creatures are natural enemies. For example, Flies and Spiders are often found at odds with one another.

&#91; 8 &#93;

Common behaviours when a creature is angry include vandalising the dungeon and deserting the player.

&#91; 8 &#93;

The creatures are varied in their statistics; some excel at certain tasks, and others refuse to perform certain tasks. Which creatures enter the dungeon depends on which rooms the player has and how large they are; most creatures have prerequisites for entering service.

&#91; 8 &#93;

Creatures require paying regularly, and when 'Payday' comes, will head for the Treasure room to collect their wages.

&#91; 14 &#93;

Other ways to obtain creatures include imprisoning and torturing them, 'scavenging' (persuaded to defect to the player) from enemy keepers, and performing certain sacrifices at the Temple.

&#91; 5 &#93;

&#91; 8 &#93;

Creatures entering via the Portal are at the lowest experience level, and must gain experience, usually by training in the training room. Training creatures increases their attributes (such as hit points) and abilities (such as which spells they can cast).

&#91; 8 &#93;

Such spells include lightning bolts, rebounding projectiles, and increasing armour.

&#91; 8 &#93;

&#91; 15 &#93;

Creatures will frequently enter combat with heroes or creatures belonging to another keeper. Each creature has a star of the colour of the keeper it belongs to above it, displaying its experience level. The star is a health meter; as a creature takes damage, the spikes turn black.

&#91; 16 &#93;

The player has the ability to possess a creature, seeing the dungeon from its  first-person perspective  and using its attacks and abilities. This is one of the spells; others include speeding creatures up, and healing them.

&#91; 8 &#93;

&#91; 17 &#93;

A world map is available and, at the beginning, the player is allocated one of the twenty regions of a fictional, idyllic country to destroy. As the player progresses through these regions, each representing a level, the areas previously conquered will appear ransacked, twisted, and evil.

&#91; 1 &#93;

The goals for each level are straightforward: they generally fall along the lines of eliminating the heroic force or destroying all other Dungeon Keepers.

&#91; 8 &#93;

The first few levels are tutorials, teaching the player the basics.

&#91; 1 &#93;

Special items are hidden throughout certain levels. Such items perform actions such as increasing the player's creatures' experience level, or revealing the map. They can reveal a hidden level, where the player must perform a specific task, and is rewarded upon completion.

&#91; 8 &#93;

Heroes will appear at various points and times, sometimes accompanied by a tunneller dwarf, who, like imps, are able to dig.

&#91; 18 &#93;

The dungeon can be protected from being breached by having the imps fortify the walls.

&#91; 10 &#93;

Heroes include giants, wizards, and  samurai  warriors.

&#91; 1 &#93;

Most levels have a 'Lord of the Land', a heavily armoured knight, who must be defeated.

&#91; 1 &#93;

&#91; 19 &#93;

In the final level, the Avatar (resembling the Avatar from

Ultima VIII  ),

&#91; 1 &#93;

&#91; 20 &#93;

the most powerful hero, appears as the Lord of the Land, and is resurrected after being defeated. He must be defeated again when he reappears with a large army.

&#91; 1 &#93;

&#91; 8 &#93;

Multiplayer with up to four players is supported via a  modem  or over a  local area network  (LAN).

&#91; 1 &#93;

&#91; 21 &#93;

The game features twenty multiplayer levels,

&#91; 8 &#93;

playable as single-player levels.

&#91; 1 &#93;

Development

[

edit

]

Dungeon Keeper  was developed by  Bullfrog Productions  under  Peter Molyneux , who wrote the game design, testbed, and the computer players and assistant.

&#91; 8 &#93;

In an interview, Molyneux explained that he came up with the ideas for  Dungeon Keeper  while sitting in a traffic jam, and become so engrossed in them that when the traffic had moved, he did not notice.

&#91; 8 &#93;

Bullfrog's chairman  Les Edgar  stated that the intention was to use the

Dungeons & Dragons

theme with "slightly weird Bullfrog humour".

&#91; 22 &#93;

Programmers Simon and Dene Carter also said  Dungeon Keeper  is a parody.

&#91; 23 &#93;

Dungeon Keeper  is a simulation game because Molyneux viewed the villain's duty as a management exercise.

&#91; 24 &#93;

Development began in November 1994 by the lead programmer, Simon Carter, and took two and a half years to develop.

&#91; 8 &#93;

The game used the engine for

Magic Carpet  , and the first version was merely scrolling around a map and entering  third-person .

&#91; 25 &#93;

The engine was built by  Glenn Corpes .

&#91; 26 &#93;

Molyneux then developed a 2D prototype containing features that made it into the final game. The prototype focused on constructing the dungeon, and included the Treasure room, Hatchery, Training room, and Dungeon Heart. The characters were designed by the artist,  Mark Healey .

&#91; 25 &#93;

The navigation system proved difficult to develop because computers of the time lacked power. Artificial intelligence was considered crucial, as the navigation and other aspects relied on it. According to co-designer Jonty Barnes, the team wanted the creatures complex without high computational costs. Healey came up with the idea of slapping creatures to make them work faster, and Barnes considered it a "great game decision".

&#91; 25 &#93;

A great deal of time was spent working on the user interface, and at one point the idea of having no interface was considered.

&#91; 25 &#93;

Carter stated that the team tried to make the sounds atmospheric and industrial so players got a sense of power. Using others' 3D sound routines proved troublesome, so he and his colleague Tony Cox wrote their own.

&#91; 23 &#93;

Dungeon Keeper  uses lighting  algorithms  used in  Magic Carpet , which provided effects such as fireballs lighting corridors.

&#91; 27 &#93;

According to Molyneux, in May 1995 publisher  Electronic Arts  told him he had to ship  Dungeon Keeper  in six and a half weeks. Though he satisfied their demands by producing another game,

Hi-Octane  , in the required time frame, his relationship with the publisher was strained.

&#91; 28 &#93;

Development on the level editor began in May 1995. The first-person view was developed in September 1995, and the creatures had shadows added, which was believed impossible at the time.

&#91; 8 &#93;

Around that time, Barnes left the project to complete a degree at the  University of East Anglia , and returned in July 1996.

&#91; 8 &#93;

&#91; 25 &#93;

In November 1995, Barrie Parker began writing levels, and developed content for the script language.

&#91; 8 &#93;

Bullfrog tried to get a version out for Christmas 1995: it was originally scheduled for release in November 1995, and Molyneux, until September, believed that it would be ready by Christmas.

&#91; 8 &#93;

&#91; 29 &#93;

He explained that it was mostly complete, but wanted to make sure that it was "absolutely brilliant".

&#91; 29 &#93;

It was then the development team realised the game should focus on a living world created by the player.

&#91; 8 &#93;

A December 1995

PC Zone

preview reported a feature that would have let the computer take control of the player's dungeon after logging out of an internet multiplayer game.

&#91; 30 &#93;

In February 1996, Molyneux decided to focus on the project full-time.

&#91; 8 &#93;

According to Carter, the creatures were made from  bitmaps , and there was around 90 megabytes of graphics data on screen as of April 1996.

&#91; 31 &#93;

In July 1996, Alex Peters joined the project and ported the game to Windows 95.  Dungeon Keeper  was shown at the  European Computer Trade Show  in September 1996,

&#91; 8 &#93;

and was scheduled for release in December.

&#91; 29 &#93;

It became evident that the deadline would be missed, and the release was pushed back to March 1997.

&#91; 29 &#93;

The final testing began in April 1997, and  Dungeon Keeper  was signed off in June.

&#91; 8 &#93;

Dungeon Keeper  was released by Electronic Arts for Windows 95 and  MS-DOS  in North America on 26 June 1997,

&#91; 32 &#93;

&#91; 33 &#93;

&#91; 34 &#93;

and in Europe and Australia on 3 July.

&#91; 35 &#93;

&#91; 36 &#93;

Sega Saturn  and  Sony PlayStation  versions were in development and due for release in 1997,

&#91; 23 &#93;

&#91; 37 &#93;

but cancelled.

&#91; 38 &#93;

An internet version was in development, and would have supported up to 250 players.

&#91; 39 &#93;

Carter wrote and organised 800,000 lines of code.

&#91; 8 &#93;

Richard Ridings  provided voice-overs such as the overworld mentor.

&#91; 11 &#93;

&#91; 40 &#93;

&#91; 41 &#93;

&#91; 42 &#93;

The music was composed by  Russell Shaw  and, according to Healey, this came late in development.

&#91; 25 &#93;

The prospect of working on  Dungeon Keeper  motivated artist and former Bullfrog employee  Gary Carr  to return from  The Bitmap Brothers  (he had left Bullfrog because of a disagreement with Molyneux on

Theme Park  ),

&#91; 43 &#93;

but he was assigned to

Theme Hospital

instead.

&#91; 44 &#93;

Healey produced 90 per cent of the graphics.

&#91; 43 &#93;

Most of the creatures were the team members' ideas, although Molyneux was involved with the Imps.

&#91; 45 &#93;

Healey designed the Horned Reaper (who is based on an ex-girlfriend of his),

&#91; 46 &#93;

who ultimately become the game's mascot.

&#91; 45 &#93;

According to Molyneux, each character takes 1024 bytes of memory.

&#91; 27 &#93;

The game as it ended up was developed mostly in the final few months: the team started again from scratch long into the development cycle because they felt it played like

Command & Conquer  .

&#91; 47 &#93;

Before the redesign, instead of having Imps dig the gold and minions entering through portals, the game had the player dig gold and use it and  mana  to create minions.

&#91; 48 &#93;

The redesign included the replacement of the 3D graphics engine with a 2D one and the rewriting of the user interface.

&#91; 48 &#93;

Molyneux was impressed with the creature's navigation code: he stated that it is the largest piece of code, and that players could change the map repeatedly and creatures will still find routes to their destinations.

&#91; 49 &#93;

Molyneux also liked the fact that the game adapts to the player, enabling the choice of the preferred methods and strategies,

&#91; 49 &#93;

and stated that his favourite part of the game is how the player digs out rather than build up, meaning the player has heavy involvement in building the level.

&#91; 45 &#93;

He stated that he was proud of  Dungeon Keeper  ' s concept, but remarked that doing original things takes time.

&#91; 48 &#93;

He also liked that people were unable to describe  Dungeon Keeper  in any particular way.

&#91; 8 &#93;

Molyneux disliked the user interface, saying the team overdid the control panel, and commented that it was a reminder that the player was playing a video game. This was a key reason the user interface of his next video game,

Black & White  , did not feature  panels ,  buttons , or  icons .

&#91; 50 &#93;

Molyneux also disliked the lack of opponent personality, and some features being hidden too well, including (according to him) a cheat code that made the mistress creature naked.

&#91; 51 &#93;

In a 2000  Game Developers Conference  demonstration of  Black & White , he stated that it remained undiscovered.

&#91; 51 &#93;

Dungeon Keeper  was Molyneux's final project with Bullfrog before he left in July 1997 to form  Lionhead Studios ,

&#91; 52 &#93;

and after completion, he stated that he wanted to make " the coolest game ever ".

&#91; 8 &#93;

Molyneux decided to leave Bullfrog in July 1996 due to frustration over corporate meetings and other management responsibilities that ensued after it was sold to Electronic Arts in 1995. He intended to leave as soon as  Dungeon Keeper  was complete,

&#91; 53 &#93;

and his departure was one reason he wanted to make the game good.

&#91; 8 &#93;

As a result of Molyneux's decision, Electronic Arts asked him to leave its offices, but Molyneux wanted to continue development, so the team moved into his house.

&#91; 25 &#93;

Molyneux, Mark Webley, Tim Rance, and  Steve Jackson  made the decision to found Lionhead when  Dungeon Keeper  was nearing completion.

&#91; 52 &#93;

In 2017, Molyneux expressed regret at leaving Bullfrog, and explained that he was drunk when he handed his notice in, and that it was "such a silly thing to do".

&#91; 54 &#93;

A sequel,

Dungeon Keeper 2

was released in 1999,

&#91; 55 &#93;

and the series was rebooted by  Mythic Entertainment  with the release of a remake (also titled

Dungeon Keeper  ) for  iOS  and  Android  on 30 January 2014.

&#91; 56 &#93;

SoundFont support

[

edit

]

The game uses SoundFonts to provide additional ambient sound effects. A  Sound Blaster AWE32  or  AWE64  is required to use this feature.

&#91; 57 &#93;

The player can load a SoundFont and use it for ambient sound effects. Customised SoundFonts can be created to personalise the dungeon.

&#91; 58 &#93;

The game features three SoundFonts, one of which is loaded at game startup,

&#91; 58 &#93;

and Creative offered sample  Dungeon Keeper  SoundFonts for download.

&#91; 59 &#93;

Expansions

[

edit

]

An  expansion pack ,  The Deeper Dungeons , was released on 26 November 1997.

&#91; 60 &#93;

It features fifteen new levels each for single player and multiplayer, and an improved  artificial intelligence  for the enemies.

&#91; 61 &#93;

The Avatar also reappears in the final level.

&#91; 1 &#93;

A Direct3D version, which brought improved graphics and  3D acceleration  support, and a level editor were released for the game.

&#91; 61 &#93;

KeeperFX , an abbreviation of "Fan eXpansion" was released in 2010, and has been regularly updated since. This mod increases compatibility with later versions of  Windows , fixes bugs, and adds features such as  TCP/IP  multiplayer support and higher screen resolution options.

&#91; 62 &#93;

&#91; 63 &#93;

&#91; 64 &#93;

Re-releases

[

edit

]

The game was re-released in 1998 as  Dungeon Keeper Gold Edition , which included the  Deeper Dungeons  expansion as well as a  Dungeon Keeper -based  desktop theme .

&#91; 65 &#93;

In Japan, a similar edition was released as  Dungeon Keeper Premium ,

&#91; a &#93;

under the EA Best Selection brand.

&#91; 66 &#93;

This version included the content from the  Gold  release, as well as both Japanese and English text and audio options.

&#91; 66 &#93;

The  Gold  version was re-released into  digital distribution  on  GOG.com  in June 2011.

&#91; 67 &#93;

GOG.com launched a  macOS  version in October 2012.

&#91; 68 &#93;

The game was available there free of charge for a few days in February 2014.

&#91; 69 &#93;

In October 2016,  Dungeon Keeper  was released free of charge on  Origin ,

&#91; 70 &#93;

via its "On The House" programme.

&#91; 71 &#93;

Sales

[

edit

]

During 1997,  Dungeon Keeper  sold 113,407 copies in the United States alone.

&#91; 89 &#93;

It received a "Gold" award from the Verband der Unterhaltungssoftware Deutschland (VUD) in August 1998,

&#91; 90 &#93;

for sales of at least 100,000 units across Germany, Austria and Switzerland.

&#91; 91 &#93;

The game's global sales reached 700,000 copies by 2003. Molyneux considered it a "missed opportunity" in comparison to his earlier games such as

Theme Park  , which sold millions of units.

&#91; 92 &#93;

Impact and legacy

[

edit

]

Many reviews of video games that have similar elements mention  Dungeon Keeper  as both an influence for the designers and a standard for comparison. These include:

Lego Rock Raiders  .

&#91; 93 &#93;

Retro Gamer  , this was asserted to be "the ultimate sequel to  Dungeon Keeper ".

Startopia  .

&#91; 94 &#93;

&#91; 25 &#93;

2004's

Evil Genius  ,

&#91; 95 &#93;

the second game developed by  Elixir Studios . Players control a James Bond-style lair and construct military and scientific installations, with the goal of expansion.

&#91; 25 &#93;

2003's

Ghost Master  ,

&#91; 96 &#93;

in which players play the role of the enemy.

&#91; 25 &#93;

2007's

Holy Invasion of Privacy, Badman!  , another game in which players construct a dungeon and protect it from invading heroes.

&#91; 25 &#93;

&#91; 97 &#93;

It was described as "Dungeon Keeper meets  Dig Dug ".

&#91; 97 &#93;

2011's

Dungeons

but with focus on care of the heroes instead of the monsters.

&#91; 98 &#93;

2011's  Dungeon Overlord , an Adobe Flash application via the social-networking website Facebook.

&#91; 99 &#93;
