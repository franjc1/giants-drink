Dragon Ball Z: Super Butōden 3

&#91; a &#93;

is a 1994  fighting

video game  developed by  Tose  and published by  Bandai  for the  Super Nintendo Entertainment System . Based upon  Akira Toriyama 's

Dragon Ball

franchise, it is the sequel to

Dragon Ball Z: Super Butōden 2  , which was released earlier in 1993 for SNES.

&#91; 1 &#93;

Following the Majin Buu arc, its gameplay remains relatively the same as the previous two  Super Butōden  entries, consisting of one-on-one fights using a main six-button configuration, featuring special moves as well as two playable modes.

Dragon Ball Z: Super Butōden 3  was created by most of the same team who previously worked on the first two  Super Butōden  entries on Super Nintendo, with producer Toshihiro Suzuki returning to lead its production, with additional support from staff of both  Toei Animation  and

V Jump

magazine. Like its predecessors,  Super Butōden 3  garnered positive reception from critics, however the lack of a story mode was heavily criticized by reviewers.

Gameplay

[

edit

]

A match between  Android 18  and  Dabura

See also:  Dragon Ball Z: Super Butōden 2 §&#160;Gameplay

Like previous  Super Butōden  titles,  Dragon Ball Z: Super Butōden 3  is a fighting game similar to

Street Fighter II: The World Warrior  .

&#91; 2 &#93;

Players fight against other characters in one-on-one matches and the fighter who manages to deplete the health bar of the opponent wins the bout and becomes the winner of the match.

&#91; 3 &#93;

&#91; 4 &#93;

The game features two modes of play (versus and tournament) but missing from the  Super Butōden 2  is a story mode.

&#91; 2 &#93;

&#91; 5 &#93;

&#91; 6 &#93;

&#91; 7 &#93;

The roster consists of nine playable characters and their respective transformations, with an additional character being unlockable via cheat code.

&#91; 2 &#93;

Characters available are  Goku ,  Goten ,  Trunks  (both in his future and younger forms),  Majin Vegeta ,  Gohan ,  Supreme Kai ,  Majin Buu ,  Dabura  and  Android 18 .

&#91; 2 &#93;

&#91; 4 &#93;

&#91; 5 &#93;

&#91; 8 &#93;

Super Butōden 3  uses a customizable six-button control scheme;

&#91; 3 &#93;

&#91; 5 &#93;

special moves and meteor combos are executed via button combinations.

&#91; 3 &#93;

&#91; 4 &#93;

&#91; 5 &#93;

Characters can dash back and forth by pressing L and R respectively.

&#91; 4 &#93;

Players can also charge their Ki gauge by holding the Y and B buttons to unleash a Ki blast attack and counterattack it.

&#91; 2 &#93;

&#91; 4 &#93;

&#91; 5 &#93;

Returning from previous  Super Butōden  entries is the split-screen feature, where the view is split to keep fighters who are far away in the playfield shown on-screen while their respective positions are kept via a radar.

&#91; 2 &#93;

Development and release

[

edit

]

Dragon Ball Z: Super Butōden 3  was developed by most of the same team who previously worked on the previous two  Super Butōden  entries, with producer Toshihiro Suzuki returning to helm its production alongside assistant Takeo Isogai, who previously worked on video game adaptations of

Rokudenashi Blues

and  Game Boy  titles.

&#91; 8 &#93;

&#91; 9 &#93;

Both music and sound design were handled by Akihito "Switch. E" Suita,  Kenji Yamamoto  and Shinji "Amayang" Amagishi.

&#91; 9 &#93;

&#91; 10 &#93;

Staff from both Toei Animation and  V Jump  magazine also assisted with its development.

&#91; 9 &#93;

&#91; 11 &#93;

When comparing to  Super Butōden 2 , Isogai stated in a 1994 interview with French magazine

Consoles +

&#160;&#91; fr &#93;  that the visuals were improved, character animations were reworked and adjustments to the combat system were implemented.

&#91; 8 &#93;

Isogai also stated that the team, with fifteen members, worked on  Super Butōden 3  over the course of six months.

&#91; 8 &#93;

Isogai claimed that developing animation for large  sprites  such as Dabura proved difficult, while difficulty when faithfully adapting characters from the anime series arose as well.

&#91; 8 &#93;

Isogai also claimed that the split-screen feature was difficult to manage due to the character's body extremities disappearing on-screen.

&#91; 8 &#93;

Dragon Ball Z: Super Butōden 3  was first released by Bandai in Japan on September 29, 1994 and later in Europe on January 25, 1995 under the title  Dragon Ball Z: Ultime Menace .

&#91; 12 &#93;

&#91; 13 &#93;

Takeo Isogai claimed that due to the  Dragon Ball Z  anime not being broadcast in the United States, United Kingdom and Germany, the game would not sell in those countries and its release in Europe was limited to France, Italy and Spain.

&#91; 8 &#93;

Prior to launch, the game was showcased by Bandai at the 1994 Tokyo Toy Fair.

&#91; 14 &#93;

On October 21, 1994, an album was published exclusively in Japan by  Columbia , featuring arranged songs composed by Yamamoto.

&#91; 10 &#93;

&#91; 15 &#93;

&#91; 16 &#93;

On November 7, 1994, an official strategy guide was released in Japan by  Shueisha , featuring Suzuki explaining the gameplay system.

&#91; 3 &#93;

Dragon Ball Z: Super Butōden 3  received positive reception from critics and players.

&#91; 18 &#93;

&#91; 23 &#93;

&#91; 24 &#93;

In a poll taken by  Family Computer Magazine , the game received a score of 21.9 out of 30, indicating a popular following.

&#91; 25 &#93;

The title also sold approximately 910,000 copies in Japan.

&#91; 26 &#93;

Olivier Prézeau and Grégoire Hellot of

Joypad

&#160;&#91; fr &#93;  praised the new characters, music and animation speed, however they criticized the lack of story mode and technical innovations compared to  Dragon Ball Z: Super Butōden 2 .

&#91; 6 &#93;

Super Play

' s Tony Mott noted it to be similar to  Super Butōden 2 , praising both visuals and gameplay but felt mixed in regards to the sound and overall longevity.

&#91; 21 &#93;

Likewise, Christophe Delpierre of

Nintendo Player

&#160;&#91; fr &#93;  and

Player One

&#160;&#91; fr &#93;  commended the graphical presentation, speed, sound design and controls but also noted similarities with the previous game, stating that "This new DBZ is far from bad, but it innovates too little compared to its predecessor."

&#91; 22 &#93;

&#91; 20 &#93;

Hobby Consolas

' s Sonia Herranz gave positive remarks to the audiovisual presentation and gameplay, however it was criticized for the lack of a story mode and innovations compared to previous  Super Butōden  entries.

&#91; 5 &#93;

In a similar manner,

Nintendo Acción

' s Javier Abad praised the manga-like visuals, sound design and addition of new characters but criticized the lack of story mode.

&#91; 2 &#93;

Jeuxvideo.com

' s Rroyd-Y commended the visuals quality of both characters and stages, playability and music but criticized the lack of story mode as well, stating that "While its predecessor turned out to be excellent, the last game in this trilogy is a huge disappointment. A sad result for what was to be the ultimate  Dragon Ball Z  experience on Super Nintendo."

&#91; 7 &#93;
