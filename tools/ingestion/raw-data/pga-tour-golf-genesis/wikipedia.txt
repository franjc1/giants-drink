PGA Tour Golf

is a  golf

video game  and the first in the

PGA Tour

game series. It was developed by Sterling Silver Software and released in 1990 for  MS-DOS . It was initially published by  Electronic Arts , which subsequently released versions of the game for  Sega Genesis  and  Amiga  in 1991, followed by a version for the  SNES  in 1992. By 1994,  Tengen  had published versions for Sega's  Master System  and  Game Gear  consoles.  PGA Tour Golf  received generally positive reviews for its realism, sound, and camera. Several critics considered the computer versions to be the best golf game available at the time of its release. It was followed by

PGA Tour Golf II  .

Gameplay

[

edit

]

PGA Tour Golf  features three real golf courses:  PGA West ,  TPC Avenel , and  TPC Sawgrass . It also includes a fictional fourth course, Sterling Shores.

&#91; 4 &#93;

&#91; 5 &#93;

&#91; 6 &#93;

Various game modes are featured, including Tournament,  Driving ,  Putting , and Practice.

&#91; 5 &#93;

&#91; 7 &#93;

In Tournament, the player competes against a total of 60 unseen players, whose statistics are displayed on a scoreboard.

&#91; 8 &#93;

&#91; 9 &#93;

The player can watch a replay of any golf shot, and good shots are automatically replayed.

&#91; 10 &#93;

&#91; 9 &#93;

The game includes variable wind conditions which affect where the ball will land.

&#91; 8 &#93;

&#91; 7 &#93;

&#91; 10 &#93;

Before starting each course, the player is given a three-dimensional view of the hole, and the camera pans from the hole to the player's starting position.

&#91; 8 &#93;

&#91; 11 &#93;

To further aid the player, an overhead map of the course is shown before starting each hole and after each shot.

&#91; 8 &#93;

Upon reaching the  green , the player views the area as a three-dimensional contoured grid to study the uneven terrain for better putting.

&#91; 8 &#93;

&#91; 9 &#93;

The player also receives advice from  PGA  golfers.

&#91; 8 &#93;

&#91; 6 &#93;

&#91; 12 &#93;

The  Amiga  and  Master System  versions include a  multiplayer  option.

&#91; 8 &#93;

&#91; 4 &#93;

A 1994 U.K. re-release of the Amiga version includes the three courses from

PGA Tour Golf II  , as well as three additional tournaments.

&#91; 13 &#93;

&#91; 14 &#93;

Development and release

[

edit

]

PGA Tour Golf  was developed by American company Sterling Silver Software, and was initially published by  Electronic Arts ,

&#91; 9 &#93;

&#91; 1 &#93;

which released the game for  MS-DOS  computers in July 1990.

&#91; 15 &#93;

The following year, Electronic Arts released versions for Amiga and  Sega Genesis  (also known as the Mega Drive).

&#91; 16 &#93;

&#91; 1 &#93;

&#91; 12 &#93;

The Genesis version was released in the U.S. in April 1991,

&#91; 2 &#93;

followed by a U.K. release the next month.

&#91; 1 &#93;

A  Macintosh  version had also been released in the U.S. by the end of 1991.

&#91; 17 &#93;

In March 1992, Electronics Arts published a U.S. version for the  SNES .

&#91; 18 &#93;

It was also released in the U.K.

&#91; 19 &#93;

By 1994,  Tengen  had published versions for Sega's  Game Gear  and Master System consoles.

&#91; 5 &#93;

&#91; 20 &#93;

&#91; 4 &#93;

&#91; 3 &#93;

The latter version is a conversion of the Genesis version, which was converted from the computer version.

&#91; 4 &#93;

In the U.K., The Hit Squad published re-releases for the Amiga in 1994 and 1996,

&#91; 21 &#93;

&#91; 22 &#93;

while a 1997 Amiga re-release was published in the U.K. by Guildhall.

&#91; 23 &#93;

The game's music was composed by  Rob Hubbard .

&#91; 10 &#93;

Amiga Action

stated that  PGA Tour Golf  stood out from other golfing games with its various features, while concluding that it could appeal to non golf fans.

&#91; 27 &#93;

Phil Campbell of  Australian Commodore and Amiga Review  believed the game was better than

Mean 18  , with superior graphics, improved views of the course, and more game options.

&#91; 33 &#93;

The One  , reviewing the DOS version in 1990, stated that the game had a perfect balance of realism and simplistic gameplay.

&#91; 9 &#93;

The One  reviewed the Amiga version of  PGA Tour Golf  in 1991, giving it an overall score of 92%. They called it a "brilliant conversion" of the PC version, and further expressed that it "has retained the energy and playability of the original PC version."  The One  criticises the 'fly-by' feature as 'jerky', as well as the game's frequent disk-accessing, although noting that the latter can be disabled though settings, and expresses that these grievances cause "nothing major [to be] lost from the gameplay."  The One  praises  PGA Tour Golf's  sound effects and attention to detail, furthermore stating that " PGA Tour Golf  is  the  best golf game currently available."

&#91; 15 &#93;

Sandra Foley of

Amiga Computing

stated that Electronic Arts "managed to come closer than any other to simulating actual golf." However, she criticized the lack of female golfers, the bland graphics, the minimal sounds, and the inclusion of "one of the worst loading tunes I've ever heard."

&#91; 16 &#93;

Amiga User International

praised the replay modes and considered the game superior to

Leader Board  , but criticized Hubbard's musical score as a "grating series of fanfares".

&#91; 10 &#93;

Amiga Power

considered the game easy and addictive, and called it a worthy successor to  Leader Board , but noted that it did not introduce any new features among golfing video games.

&#91; 8 &#93;

ACE

praised the game and stated that it would appeal to experienced and inexperienced golf players.

&#91; 24 &#93;

Raze

reviewed the Amiga and Genesis versions; their presentation was praised, but the Genesis version was considered superior for being easier.

&#91; 6 &#93;

Mean Machines

praised the Genesis version, including its multiplayer option. The magazine concluded that it was superior to

Arnold Palmer Tournament Golf  , and stated that it would appeal to golfers as well as people who normally would not purchase golf games.

&#91; 1 &#93;

VideoGames & Computer Entertainment

praised the realism of the Genesis version and considered it an impressive conversion of the computer version, while calling it a "thoroughly entertaining, challenging and complex golf simulation." However, the magazine criticized the graphics, considering them inferior to  Arnold Palmer Tournament Golf .

&#91; 12 &#93;

AllGame 's Jonathan Sutyak praised the Genesis version for its realism, but felt that the graphics could be better.

&#91; 25 &#93;

Reviewers for

Electronic Gaming Monthly

praised the sounds of the Genesis version but had varying opinions about the graphics;

&#91; 2 &#93;

Jonathan Davies, writing for the U.K. magazine

Super Play  , criticized the SNES version for its "horrible" colors, jerky camera scrolling, and unrealistic ball movements, but stated that it played as well as the computer versions and was enjoyable. Davies concluded that it was not the best SNES golf game but that it was a "reasonable stop-gap until something better makes it over to this country."

&#91; 19 &#93;

N-Force

considered the swing animation in the SNES version to be somewhat jerky, and stated that the swing control method was inconsistent and frustrating. The magazine criticized the bland graphics and minimal backgrounds, but praised the fast and smooth animation of the ball while it is in flight to the hole.

&#91; 31 &#93;

Mean Machines  praised the Master System version, considering it the console's best golf game. The magazine stated that it played as good as other versions of the game, and considered it superior to the SNES version, although slow screen reanimation was criticized.

&#91; 4 &#93;

GamePro  stated that the Game Gear version mostly fulfilled its goal to provide good golfing on the console, but considered it an average game. The magazine criticized the minimal sounds and music, and stated that the courses looked too similar to each other.

&#91; 5 &#93;

Game Players

stated that the Game Gear version had relatively good graphics but poor gameplay, criticizing unpredictable and inaccurate shooting distances. The magazine stated that the Game Gear version was not as good as the Genesis version.

&#91; 20 &#93;

Mega  , in its October 1992 issue, placed  PGA Tour Golf  at number 10 on its list of Top Mega Drive Games of All Time.

&#91; 35 &#93;

In 1994,

PC Gamer UK

named  PGA Tour Golf  the 48th best computer game of all time. The editors wrote, "It may be old, but games as good as this deserve a look-in even ten years down the line".
