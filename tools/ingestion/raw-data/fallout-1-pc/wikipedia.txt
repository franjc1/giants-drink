Fallout

(also known as

Fallout: A Post Nuclear Role Playing Game  ) is a 1997  role-playing video game  developed and published by  Interplay Productions , set in a mid-22nd century  post-apocalyptic  and  retro-futuristic  world, decades after a global  nuclear war  led by the United States and China.  Fallout  's  protagonist, the Vault Dweller, inhabits an underground nuclear shelter. The player must scour the surrounding wasteland for a computer chip that can fix the Vault's failed water supply system. They interact with other survivors, some of whom give them  quests , and engage in  turn-based  combat.

Tim Cain  began working on  Fallout  in 1994. It began and was conceptualized as based on the role-playing game

GURPS ,  but after  Steve Jackson Games  objected to  Fallout  's  violence, Cain and designer  Christopher Taylor  created a new character customization scheme, SPECIAL. Interplay initially gave the game little attention, but eventually spent $3 million and employed up to thirty people to develop it. Interplay considered  Fallout  the spiritual successor to its 1988 role-playing game

Wasteland

and drew artistic inspiration from 1950s literature and media emblematic of the  Atomic Age  as well as the films

Mad Max

and

A Boy and His Dog  . The quests were intentionally made  morally ambiguous . After three and a half years of development,  Fallout  was released in North America in October 1997.

Fallout  received acclaim for its open-ended gameplay, character system, plot, and setting. It won "Role-Playing Game of the Year" from

GameSpot

and

Computer Games Magazine

and was nominated by the  Academy of Interactive Arts & Sciences  at the  Spotlight Awards .  Fallout  was a commercial success, selling more than half a million copies worldwide. Often listed among the  greatest video games of all time ,  Fallout  has been credited for renewing consumer interest in the role-playing video game genre. It spawned the widely successful

Fallout  series , the rights to which were purchased in 2007 by  Bethesda Softworks .

Gameplay

[

edit

]

Character creation

[

edit

]

Fallout  is a  role-playing video game . The player begins by selecting one of three  characters , or one with player-customized  attributes .

&#91; 2 &#93;

The protagonist, known as the Vault Dweller,

&#91; b &#93;

has seven primary statistics that the player can set: strength, perception, endurance, charisma, intelligence, agility, and luck.

&#91; 6 &#93;

Each statistic may range from one to ten, provided their sum does not exceed 40.

&#91; 7 &#93;

Two other statistics set during  character creation  are skills and traits.

&#91; 8 &#93;

All 18 skills are learned abilities, their effectiveness determined by a percentage value. Their initial effectivenesses are determined by the primary statistics, but three can be tagged and given a 20% boost.

&#91; 9 &#93;

Traits are character qualities with both a positive and negative effect; the player can pick up to two from a list of sixteen.

&#91; 10 &#93;

&#91; 11 &#93;

During gameplay, the player can gather  experience points  through various actions. For gathering experience points, the player will  level up  and may increase their skills by a set number of points.

&#91; 8 &#93;

Every three (or four with the Skilled trait) levels, the player can grant themself a special ability, known as perks.

&#91; 12 &#93;

There are 48 perks (plus two extra perks which can only be obtained by wearing certain types of armor) and each has prerequisites that must be met. For example, "Animal Friend", which prevents animals from attacking the player character, requires the player to be level nine, have an intelligence of five or higher, and have an outdoorsman skill of 25%.

&#91; 13 &#93;

Exploration and combat

[

edit

]

Dialogue with a non-player character with a talking head, in which the player is offered quests to complete

In  Fallout , the player explores the game world from a  trimetric perspective  and interacts with  non-player characters  (NPCs). Characters vary in their amount of dialogue; some say short messages, while others speak at length. Significant characters are illustrated with  3D models , known as "talking heads", during conversations.

&#91; 10 &#93;

&#91; 14 &#93;

The player can barter with other characters or buy goods using bottle caps as currency.

&#91; 15 &#93;

The game has companions that the player can recruit for exploration and combat, although they cannot be directly controlled.

&#91; 10 &#93;

There are three main  quests  where completion is required, two of them given after completion of the first one. The first main quest has a time limit of 150 in-game days; the  game ends  if the player fails to complete it within the allotted time.

&#91; 16 &#93;

&#91; 17 &#93;

&#91; c &#93;

Some characters give the player side quests; if the player solves them, they receive experience points and occasionally a reward in the form of money and/or goods.

&#91; 8 &#93;

&#91; 16 &#93;

The player can utilize the  PIP-Boy 2000 , a portable  wearable computer  that tracks these quests.

&#91; 19 &#93;

Many quests feature multiple solutions; they can often be completed through diplomacy, combat, or stealth, and some allow solutions that are unconventional or contrary to the original task.

&#91; 20 &#93;

Based on how they completed quests, the player can earn or lose  reputation  points, which determine how others treat them.

&#91; 10 &#93;

The player's actions dictate what future story or gameplay opportunities are available and the ending.

&#91; 16 &#93;

&#91; 21 &#93;

&#91; 8 &#93;

Combat is  turn based  and uses an  action-point  system, the number of action points that are available depending on certain perks and the player's allocation in the agility statistic. During each turn, multiple actions may be performed by the player until they run out of action points.

&#91; 22 &#93;

Different actions such as attacking, moving, reloading, interacting with objects mid-combat, and accessing the inventory consume different amounts of points.

&#91; 23 &#93;

The player can rapidly switch between two equipped weapons,

&#91; 24 &#93;

and may acquire a diverse range of weapons,

&#91; 16 &#93;

many of which can target specific areas of enemies.

&#91; 25 &#93;

All weapons have one or two types of attacks, for example: Melee ( hand-to-hand ) weapons typically have two attacks: swing or thrust, automatic firearms come with a burst mode. If the player has equipped no weapon, they can punch or kick.

&#91; 26 &#93;

Plot

[

edit

]

Setting

[

edit

]

On October 23, 2077, a worldwide  nuclear war  between the United States and China following a global conflict caused by resource shortages devastated the world and destroyed modern civilization. The events of  Fallout  take place nearly a century later in 2161, and follow the Vault Dweller, a human born and raised within Vault 13, one of a number of high-tech underground  fallout shelters  built to protect survivors.

&#91; 27 &#93;

&#91; 28 &#93;

&#91; 16 &#93;

Those on the surface live off the salvage of the old world.

&#91; 16 &#93;

Vault 13 is located beneath the mountains of  Southern California .

&#91; 16 &#93;

The Vault Dweller can explore major settlements including Junktown, which is mired in conflict between local sheriff Killian Darkwater ( Richard Dean Anderson ) and criminal Gizmo ( Jim Cummings ); the Hub, a bustling merchant city with job opportunities;

&#91; 29 &#93;

and Necropolis, a city founded in the remains of  Bakersfield  by  Ghouls , humans mutated by radiation, who are revealed to be the former inhabitants of Vault 12.

&#91; 27 &#93;

The Vault Dweller's journey also brings them into contact with various factions, including the  Brotherhood of Steel , a quasi-religious military order devoted to finding and restoring pre-war technology;

&#91; 27 &#93;

&#91; 30 &#93;

the Children of the Cathedral, an optimistic religious cult;

&#91; 31 &#93;

and an army of virtually immortal humanoids immune to radiation called  Super Mutants , led by an amalgamated creature known as "the Master".

&#91; 27 &#93;

Characters

[

edit

]

The player controls the Vault Dweller, who is sent into the Wasteland to save their vault. The Vault Dweller can be customized or based on one of three pre-generated characters: Albert Cole, a negotiator and charismatic leader with a legal background; Natalia Dubrovhsky, a talented acrobat and the intelligent and resourceful granddaughter of a Russian diplomat in the pre-War  Soviet  consulate in Los Angeles; and Max Stone, the largest person in the Vault who is known for his strength, stamina, and lack of intelligence.

&#91; 32 &#93;

The three characters present a diplomatic, deceptive, or combative approach to the game, respectively.

&#91; 12 &#93;

Although the character can be male or female, the Vault Dweller is canonically male.

&#91; 4 &#93;

&#91; 33 &#93;

The four companions the player can recruit are: Ian, a mercenary guard from Shady Sands;

&#91; 34 &#93;

Tycho, a desert ranger;

&#91; 4 &#93;

Dogmeat , a tireless loyal dog;

&#91; 35 &#93;

and Katja, a member of an organization called the Followers of the Apocalypse.

&#91; 36 &#93;

Other major characters include  Vault Boy , the mascot of Vault-Tec,

&#91; 37 &#93;

the creators of the Vaults;

&#91; 38 &#93;

Killian Darkwater, the mayor, sheriff, and shopkeeper of Junktown;

&#91; 22 &#93;

&#91; 29 &#93;

and the Master (Cummings and  Kath Soucie ), leader of the Super Mutants and the main  antagonist .

&#91; 39 &#93;

&#91; 40 &#93;

Plot

[

edit

]

In Vault 13, the Water Chip, a computer component responsible for the Vault's water recycling and pumping machinery, stops working. With only 150 days before water reserves will run dry, the Vault Overseer (Ken Mars) tasks the Vault Dweller with finding a replacement. Armed with the PIPBoy 2000 and meager equipment, the Vault Dweller leaves Vault 13 for the nearest source of possible help, Vault 15, but finds it abandoned and in ruins.

&#91; 41 &#93;

The Vault Dweller then explores the wasteland and locates a replacement chip in the destroyed Vault 12, underneath Necropolis.

The Vault Dweller returns to Vault 13 with the chip and the water system is repaired. However, the Overseer becomes concerned about the mutants reported by the Vault Dweller. Believing the mutations are too widespread and extreme to be a natural occurrence, the Overseer assigns the Vault Dweller a new task: finding and stopping the source of the mutations.

&#91; 42 &#93;

Information discovered throughout the wasteland reveals that humans are being captured and turned into Super Mutants by exposure to the Forced Evolutionary Virus (F.E.V.). The Super Mutants are led by the Master, who intends to transform every human into a Super Mutant and establish "unity" on Earth. The Children of the cathedral are a front created by the Master, who is using them to trick humans into peaceful submission.

To stop the mutations, the Vault Dweller must destroy the vats containing the F.E.V. and kill the Master; the order of the tasks is chosen by the player.

&#91; 15 &#93;

The Vault Dweller travels to the Mariposa Military Base to destroy it and the vats within, preventing the creation of more Super Mutants. To kill the Master, the Vault Dweller travels to the Children's Cathedral and locates a prototype Vault beneath it, from which the Master commands his army. The Vault Dweller infiltrates the Vault and can choose to convince the Master that his plan will fail because the Super Mutants are infertile, kill him immediately, or set off an explosion that destroys the cathedral.

&#91; 39 &#93;

The Vault Dweller returns to Vault 13 but is denied entry by the Overseer, who fears that they have been changed by their experiences and the tales of their exploits and accomplishments will encourage the inhabitants to abandon the Vault. As such, the Overseer reluctantly exiles the Vault Dweller into the wasteland.

&#91; 5 &#93;

&#91; 43 &#93;

Fallout  concludes with the legacy of the Vault Dweller's decisions on the societies and people they had encountered.

&#91; 44 &#93;

Additionally, there is an alternate ending if the player chooses to join the Master. This ending has the player character dunked into the F.E.V. and made into a super mutant, who then returns to Vault 13 with the Master's army and massacres its inhabitants as witnessed on security footage.

Development

[

edit

]

Tim Cain (pictured in 2010) was the creator, producer, and one of the programmers of  Fallout .

Development on  Fallout  began in early 1994.

&#91; 45 &#93;

&#91; 46 &#93;

Initially, Interplay gave the game little attention,

&#91; 47 &#93;

and for the first six months, the programmer  Tim Cain  was the sole developer. Cain eventually enlisted the aid of Interplay employees during their spare time.

&#91; 46 &#93;

The development team—led by Cain, the designer  Christopher Taylor , and the art director  Leonard Boyarsky

&#91; 4 &#93;

—reached 15 people in 1995. In 1996, the producer  Feargus Urquhart  recruited some Interplay workers, expanding the team to 30.

&#91; 46 &#93;

&#91; 48 &#93;

&#91; 49 &#93;

Cain considered the team "amazing" for their dedication,

&#91; 50 &#93;

while Urquhart described working under Interplay as "barely controlled chaos".

&#91; 51 &#93;

The tentative title,  Vault-13: A GURPS Post-Nuclear Role-Playing Game , was rejected as unfitting.  Armageddon  was considered as an alternative, but was already in use for another Interplay project (which was later canceled).

&#91; 4 &#93;

Interplay's president  Brian Fargo  suggested the title  Fallout .

&#91; 46 &#93;

Interplay intended to use " I Don't Want to Set the World on Fire " by  the Ink Spots  as the theme song, but was hindered by a  copyright  problem, so used another Ink Spots song, " Maybe ".

&#91; 4 &#93;

&#91; 52 &#93;

&#91; 53 &#93;

The development concluded on October 1, 1997,

&#91; 54 &#93;

after three and a half years and a total cost of approximately $3 &#160; million (~$5.43&#160;million in 2024).

&#91; 55 &#93;

&#91; 56 &#93;

Engine and design

[

edit

]

POV-Ray  render mimicking  Fallout  ' s  oblique projection  and  hexagonal grid

Fallout  started as a  game engine  that Cain was developing during his spare time, based on the tabletop role-playing game

Generic Universal RolePlaying System

( GURPS ). It entered more coordinated development after Cain convinced Fargo of its potential,

&#91; 57 &#93;

&#91; 58 &#93;

and Interplay announced it had acquired the  GURPS  license in 1994.

&#91; 59 &#93;

The first  Fallout  prototype was finished that year.

&#91; 60 &#93;

The team considered making the game  first-person  and 3D, but discarded the idea because the models would not have held the desired amount of detail.

&#91; 51 &#93;

&#91; 49 &#93;

They instead selected an  oblique projection , producing a trimetric perspective.

&#91; 49 &#93;

Designed to be open-world and non-linear,

&#91; 61 &#93;

Fallout  was  balanced  so that, even though side quests are optional, characters who do not improve their skills and experience through them would be too ill-equipped to finish.

&#91; 59 &#93;

However, Taylor also added the 150-day time limit to the game to keep the player focused on the main quests.

&#91; 39 &#93;

The game was nearly canceled in late 1994 after Interplay acquired the licenses to the

Dungeons & Dragons

franchises

Forgotten Realms

and

Planescape  , but Cain convinced Interplay to let him finish.

&#91; 62 &#93;

After the success of the role-playing video game

Diablo  , released in January 1997,

&#91; 63 &#93;

Cain resisted pressure to convert  Fallout  into a real-time  multiplayer  game.

&#91; 46 &#93;

In March 1997, Interplay dropped the license for  GURPS  due to creative differences with  GURPS  's  creator  Steve Jackson Games . According to Interplay,  Steve Jackson  objected to the amount of violence and gore.

&#91; 46 &#93;

&#91; 64 &#93;

&#91; 65 &#93;

Interplay was forced to change the  GURPS  system to the internally-developed SPECIAL system;

&#91; 64 &#93;

Taylor and Cain were each given a week to design and code it, respectively.

&#91; 46 &#93;

&#91; 66 &#93;

Concept and influences

[

edit

]

Leonard Boyarsky (pictured in 2017) was the art director of  Fallout .

Prior to the license's termination, the engine for  Fallout  was based on  GURPS . Fantasy and time-traveling settings were considered before the development team decided on a post-apocalyptic setting.

&#91; 39 &#93;

&#91; 46 &#93;

&#91; 67 &#93;

Taylor outlined the design goals in a vision statement,

&#91; 68 &#93;

which Cain called an inspiration for the development team and "a major reason why the game came together at all."

&#91; 46 &#93;

Fallout  was a spiritual successor to Interplay's role-playing video game

Wasteland

(1988), published by  Electronic Arts .

&#91; 69 &#93;

Almost everyone who worked on  Fallout  had played it.

&#91; 70 &#93;

The team was unable to make  Fallout  a direct sequel to  Wasteland  because Electronic Arts refused to license it.

&#91; 39 &#93;

The team drew inspiration for  Fallout  's

retro-futuristic  art style from 1950s literature and media related to the  Atomic Age . Examples included the films

Forbidden Planet

(1956),

A Boy and His Dog

(1975), and

Mad Max

(1979). Influence was also drawn from the optimistic  Cold War  posters, which Boyarsky reportedly loved.

&#91; 46 &#93;

&#91; 71 &#93;

&#91; 39 &#93;

The vaults were influenced by the underground base in  A Boy and His Dog .

&#91; 46 &#93;

Cain said that the team "all loved

X-COM  " and that  Fallout  featured combat similar to  X-COM  's  prior to the  GURPS  license.

&#91; 72 &#93;

Cain admired

Star Control II

(1992) and said it influenced  Fallout  's  open-ended design.

&#91; 73 &#93;

Fallout  features many popular culture references. The team was only allowed to include references if understanding the source material was not required for the reference to make sense. For example, the Slayer perk's name references the television series

Buffy the Vampire Slayer

while generically matching its effect (turning all attacks to critical hits).

&#91; 74 &#93;

Characters and writing

[

edit

]

The  sprites  in  Fallout  were highly detailed and required a significant amount of  memory  on contemporary computers.

&#91; 75 &#93;

21 NPCs were voiced by various actors, including Anderson, Cummings, Soucie,  Tony Shalhoub ,  Keith David ,  Brad Garrett ,  CCH Pounder ,  Tony Jay ,  Pamela Adlon ,  Richard Moll ,  David Warner ,  Clancy Brown ,  Kenneth Mars , and  Ron Perlman , who also voiced the narrator.

&#91; 4 &#93;

. Perlman recalled in 2026 that he had been paid "40 dollars and a sandwich" for his performance.

&#91; 76 &#93;

Some NPCs feature 3D models during conversation called "talking heads", most of which were created by Scott Redenhizer. Each took eight weeks to create, and voice recording took a few months.

&#91; 77 &#93;

The talking heads began as sculpted heads of clay, which the team studied to determine which parts should be most animated. The heads were digitized using a Faro Space Arm and  VertiSketch , with

LightWave 3D

used for geometric corrections and the  texture maps  created in  Adobe Photoshop .

&#91; 59 &#93;

Much of the spoken dialogue was written by designer Mark O'Green, whose method of writing Taylor considered efficient and impressive.

&#91; 49 &#93;

The companions, conceived late in development, were not easy to implement. As a result, they were riddled with glitches, including a tendency to shoot the Vault Dweller when they are obscuring an enemy.  Dogmeat  was the first companion added.

&#91; 78 &#93;

Tycho was a reference to the desert rangers from  Wasteland .

&#91; 4 &#93;

Cain wrote the prologue, which included the series catchphrase "War. War never changes".

&#91; 39 &#93;

Vault Boy and his cheerful nature parodied how 1950s media downplayed the perils of nuclear war.

&#91; 79 &#93;

Cain, who dislikes it when the player character knows more than the player, devised  Fallout  's  narrative so that the Vault Dweller would know just as much as the player.

&#91; 51 &#93;

The development team conceived of a faction of mutants who grew their ranks by dipping people into virus vats. During the discussion, someone wondered what would happen if more than one person was dropped into the vat. The team conceptualized the leader of the faction as a synthesis of a man, a woman, and a computer terminal mutated together. Cain enjoyed modelling, animating, and writing the Master's dialog, particularly because the Master switched between three voices: male, female, and electronic.

&#91; 80 &#93;

The development team became confident in their vision after the audio director reacted to the voice-switching concept, and every department believed the Master would be a great antagonist.

&#91; 80 &#93;

The quests in  Fallout  were given  moral ambiguity , with no clear right or wrong solutions. This was done so the player could take whatever choice suited them best.

&#91; 81 &#93;

An example is the final encounter with the Master, whose motives for establishing unity among the wasteland population and making it immune to radiation by turning them into mutants could be perceived as persuasive by the player.

&#91; 39 &#93;

&#91; 82 &#93;

&#91; 83 &#93;

Assistant designer  Scott Bennie  described the backstory of the Master, who views himself as a well-intentioned hero, as an example of their intention of "hit[ting] the player with an emotional sledgehammer as often as possible" with their story design.

&#91; 84 &#93;

&#91; 51 &#93;

Release

[

edit

]

Boyarsky and lead artist  Jason D. Anderson

&#91; 51 &#93;

created advertisements for  Fallout .

&#91; 85 &#93;

Fallout  did not have a trailer,

&#91; 86 &#93;

but a  demo  was released on April 26, 1997.

&#91; 87 &#93;

Taylor felt the demo did not demonstrate  Fallout  well.

&#91; 88 &#93;

The packaging was designed to resemble a lunch box, and the manual was designed to resemble a survival guide to reflect the game's style.

&#91; 46 &#93;

Fallout  was released on October 10, 1997,

&#91; 89 &#93;

in North America for  MS-DOS  and  Windows .

&#91; 90 &#93;

The game was later released for  Mac OS  by the Interplay division  MacPlay .

&#91; 1 &#93;

&#91; 91 &#93;

Version 1.1 was released on November 13, 1997, patching many bugs in the original release and removing the 500-day time limit. The patch was released for the Mac OS on December 11, 1997.

&#91; 90 &#93;

&#91; 18 &#93;

&#91; 92 &#93;

Fallout  was initially not released in Europe due to the player's ability to kill children in-game. Version 1.2 removed the children from  Fallout  and was released at an unspecified date in Europe.

&#91; 93 &#93;

&#91; 18 &#93;

MacPlay, which had become independent from Interplay, ported  Fallout  to  Mac OS X  in 2002 as part of its "Value Series".

&#91; 1 &#93;

&#91; 91 &#93;

Fallout  and its sequels,

Fallout 2

and

Fallout Tactics: Brotherhood of Steel  , were  bundled  as  Fallout Trilogy  in April 2009.

&#91; 94 &#93;

To mark its 20th anniversary, the game was made temporarily free on video game  digital distribution  service  Steam  on September 30, 2017.

&#91; 95 &#93;

It was also included in  Fallout Anthology  in September and October 2015

&#91; 96 &#93;

and  Fallout Legacy Collection  in October 2019.

&#91; 97 &#93;

Sales

[

edit

]

Fallout  was commercially successful, although it was not as popular as other role-playing video games such as

Baldur's Gate

and  Diablo .

&#91; 108 &#93;

&#91; 29 &#93;

It did not meet sales expectations, but developed a fan following

&#91; 109 &#93;

&#91; 110 &#93;

and sold enough copies for a sequel to be produced.

&#91; 29 &#93;

In the United States, it debuted at No. 12 on  PC Data 's computer game sales rankings for October 1997.

&#91; 111 &#93;

&#91; 112 &#93;

CNET Gamecenter

noted that the game was part of a trend of role-playing successes that month, alongside

Ultima Online

and

Lands of Lore 2: Guardians of Destiny  , and said, "If October's list is any indication, [role-playing games] are back."

&#91; 111 &#93;

Fallout  sold 53,777 copies in the US by the end of 1997.

&#91; 113 &#93;

Worldwide, over 100,000 copies were shipped by December 1997,

&#91; 114 &#93;

and  Erik Bethke  reported sales of over 120,000 copies after a year.

&#91; 115 &#93;

By March 2000, 144,000 copies had been sold in the US alone.  GameSpot  called these "very good sales, especially since the overall [worldwide] figures are likely double those amounts".

&#91; 116 &#93;

Fallout  was unpopular in the United Kingdom, where sales for it and its sequel totaled just over 50,000 combined lifetime sales by 2008.

&#91; 110 &#93;

In 2017, Fargo said in an interview that  Fallout  sold a total of 600,000 copies.

&#91; 117 &#93;

After the release of the  2024 television adaptation ,  Fallout  experienced renewed commercial success. According to data trackers Steam Charts and SteamDB respectively,  Fallout  's  player base experienced a 160% increase, peaking at 2,300 players.

&#91; 118 &#93;

&#91; 119 &#93;

Legacy

[

]

Influence

[

edit

]

Jason D. Anderson, Cain, and Boyarsky (left to right; pictured in 2015) left Interplay to form  Troika Games .

The 1990s saw a decline in the popularity of the  role-playing video game  genre as a result of stale settings and ideas,

&#91; 125 &#93;

&#91; 126 &#93;

competition with other genres,

&#91; 114 &#93;

&#91; 127 &#93;

&#91; 128 &#93;

and poor quality assurance.

&#91; 129 &#93;

Fallout  has been credited as one of several innovative role-playing games that revived the genre's popularity.

&#91; 130 &#93;

&#91; 131 &#93;

&#91; 128 &#93;

In 2000,  CNET Gamecenter  's  Mark H. Walker wrote, "The RPG genre was clearly in a slump in the mid-'90s, but &#160; ... the renaissance began when Interplay's  Fallout  hit store shelves."

&#91; 132 &#93;

Rowan Kaiser, writing for

Engadget  , called  Fallout  the "first modern role-playing game".

&#91; 133 &#93;

Fallout  's  post-apocalyptic setting was novel, as contemporary role-playing games often featured  Tolkien -inspired fantasy settings.

&#91; 21 &#93;

&#91; 29 &#93;

&#91; 110 &#93;

&#91; 134 &#93;

Fallout  also stood out for its focus on the player character, how their choices impacted the game world, and the open-world gameplay.

&#91; 21 &#93;

&#91; 82 &#93;

&#91; 133 &#93;

Matthew Byrd of

Den of Geek

wrote that  Fallout  's  departure from gameplay inspired by the tabletop role-playing game

Dungeons & Dragons  , prevalent in role-playing games at the time, made it influential.

&#91; 82 &#93;

Kaiser stated that any modern game with a morality system could be tied back to  Fallout .

&#91; 133 &#93;

At the 2012  Game Developers Conference , Cain gave a presentation about  Fallout  's  development and noted traits that were shared by subsequent role-playing games, including open-world gameplay, ambiguous morality, and perks.

&#91; 135 &#93;

Polygon

described  Fallout  as "one of the most influential games of its time."

&#91; 46 &#93;

After leaving Interplay in 1998, Cain, Boyarsky, and Anderson formed  Troika Games  and created

Arcanum: Of Steamworks and Magick Obscura

(2001). Cain considered  Fallout  a "stepping stone" to the creation of  Arcanum .

&#91; 136 &#93;

Years later, working for  Obsidian Entertainment , Cain and Boyarsky created

The Outer Worlds

(2019), a role-playing video game influenced by  Fallout .

&#91; 137 &#93;

After the  Fallout  series became popular,

Wasteland 2

(2014) was pitched by Fargo and developed by  inXile Entertainment , which Fargo founded, with a design team featuring Anderson and  Fallout  composer  Mark Morgan .  PC Gamer  found  Wasteland 2  to be more similar the first two  Fallout  games than the original  Wasteland .

&#91; 138 &#93;

&#91; 139 &#93;

Other personnel from the  Fallout  development team have worked on games that were influenced by  Fallout

such as

Neverwinter Nights 2

(2006) and

Alpha Protocol

(2008).

&#91; 140 &#93;

&#91; 141 &#93;

A feature similar to the perks in  Fallout , called "feats", was added to the third edition of  Dungeons & Dragons .

&#91; 142 &#93;

Other games with similar features, according to Cain, include

World of Warcraft

(2004) and

Oblivion

(2006).

&#91; 143 &#93;

Both

Metro 2033

(2010) and

Atom RPG

(2018) are post-apocalyptic games that were influenced by  Fallout .

&#91; 144 &#93;

&#91; 145 &#93;

Other games influenced by  Fallout  include

Deus Ex

(2000),

&#91; 146 &#93;

Dark Angel: Vampire Apocalypse

(2001),

&#91; 147 &#93;

and

Weird West

(2022).

&#91; 148 &#93;
