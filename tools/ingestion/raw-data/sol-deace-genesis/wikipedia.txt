Sol-Feace

&#91; a &#93;

is a 1990 horizontally  scrolling shooter  video game developed by  Wolf Team  and published by  Telenet Japan  for the  X68000 . Versions for the  Sega CD  and  Sega Genesis  were released later on, with the latter version renaming the game

Sol-Deace  . The player takes control of the titular starship as it must prevent a malfunctioning supercomputer from enslaving all of mankind. Gameplay involves shooting down enemies and avoiding projectiles, while collecting power capsules to increase the Sol-Feace's abilities. The Sol-Feace also has dual cannons that can fire shots diagonally.

The game's soundtrack was composed by  Motoi Sakuraba , who would later work on the

Tales

series for  Namco . Alongside Micronet's

Heavy Nova  , it was also one of the first launch titles for Sega CD in Japan, and was made as the console pack-in game in North America.  Sol-Feace  was met with mixed reviews from critics, who praised its fast-paced action, graphics and soundtrack but criticized its lack of originality and subpar quality compared to other similar games on the market.  Sunsoft  owns the rights to  Sol-Feace  after their acquisition of  Telenet Japan 's intellectual properties in 2009.

Gameplay

[

edit

]

X68000 version screenshot, showing the Sol-Feace fighting the first stage boss.

Sol-Feace  is a horizontal-scrolling shooter. Controlling the titular starship, the player is tasked with preventing a malfunctioning supercomputer before it enslaves all of mankind.

&#91; 5 &#93;

The objective of each stage is to destroy incoming enemies and avoiding their projectiles.

&#91; 5 &#93;

The Sol-Feace has a pair of dual cannons that can be modified to either point forwards, backwards or diagonally, and can be arranged parallel to each other.

&#91; 5 &#93;

Shooting small capsules will leave behind power-up items that upgrade the Sol-Feace's abilities when collected — these include homing missiles, a piercing laser, and a wide shot.

&#91; 5 &#93;

The game is composed of six stages, including mechanical bases, destroyed star vessels and the planet  Jupiter

&#91; 5 &#93;

— each of these end with a boss fight that must be defeated in order to progress, which have a weak point that must be shot at to inflict damage.

&#91; 5 &#93;

The final stage has the player fighting against the supercomputer antagonist.

&#91; 5 &#93;

Between stages are animated cutscenes that help explain the game's plot, with the Sega CD release giving these full voice acting.

&#91; 5 &#93;

Development and release

[

edit

]

Sol-Feace  was originally released for the  X68000  home computer on November 22, 1990. It was developed by Japanese studio  Wolf Team  and published by their parent company,  Telenet Japan .

&#91; 6 &#93;

Music for the game was composed by  Motoi Sakuraba ,

&#91; 7 &#93;

who later helped work on the

Tales

series for  Namco .

&#91; 8 &#93;

It was ported to the  Sega CD  in 1991 as one of the console's first launch titles in Japan, alongside Micronet's

Heavy Nova  ,

&#91; 9 &#93;

and was made the pack-in game for the North American release — the latter version was published internationally by  Sega .

&#91; 10 &#93;

A  Genesis  cartridge version was released in 1992 and published by  Renovation Products , renaming the game to  Sol-Deace .

&#91; 11 &#93;

The rights to  Sol-Feace  are owned by  Sunsoft , following their acquisition of Telenet Japan's intellectual properties in 2009.

&#91; 12 &#93;

Reception

[

edit

]

Reception

Review scores

Publication  Score

89%

&#91; 10 &#93;

Sol-Feace  was met with mixed reviews from critics; while some praised the game's visuals, fast-paced gameplay and soundtrack, others criticized its lack of originality and for being subpar compared to other shoot'em up titles available.

Computer and Video Games

magazine criticized the gameplay for lacking originality, unfavorably comparing it to games such as

Hellfire

and

Gynoug  , adding that much of the game's mechanics and features were done better on other  Sega Genesis  games beforehand.

&#91; 14 &#93;

Wizard

stated it offered nothing new to the shoot 'em up genre,

&#91; 10 &#93;

while

Retro Gamer

said it was a "below par" launch game for the console, saying it was simply the Genesis version with a new CD soundtrack "tacked-on".

&#91; 9 &#93;

GamePro

wrote for the Sega Genesis release that the power-up items weren't as impressive as those in titles such as

Thunder Force III  , while also disliking the game's short length.

&#91; 11 &#93;

In a retrospective review,

AllGame

disliked the sound effects for being mediocre and its music for being average.

&#91; 13 &#93;

In a more positive light,

Electronic Games

called the Sega CD conversion a "solid side-scrolling shooter" for its sound effects, graphics and fast action-based gameplay, adding that Sega should be commended for making the game a pack-in for the peripheral.

&#91; 15 &#93;

GamePro  liked the Sega Genesis version's detailed visuals and pumping soundtrack, as well as its stage designs.

&#91; 11 &#93;

They also wrote that the game could be a contender for a "shoot'em up award" had it existed in the magazine.

&#91; 11 &#93;

Computer and Video Games  stated that the Sega CD version was not a "total waste of money", complementing its sound effects, fast speed, music and cutscenes,

&#91; 14 &#93;

while  AllGame  thought the version's dual-cannon mechanic was an interesting gameplay concept.

&#91; 13 &#93;

AllGame  also praised its frantic gameplay and colorful visuals, concluding that it was worth trying out at the very least.

&#91; 13 &#93;
