Urban Champion

&#91; a &#93;

is a  fighting

video game  developed and published by  Nintendo  for the  Nintendo Entertainment System . It was first released for the Famicom and  Nintendo VS. System  for  arcades  in 1984, and later released for the NES in North America and Europe in 1986. It is Nintendo's first 2D fighting game, eventually followed by the 1993 Famicom game

Joy Mech Fight  . The game was poorly received, and has been retroactively regarded as one of the weaker Nintendo-published games for the NES.

Gameplay

[

edit

]

A gameplay screenshot depicting the player character on the verge of being pushed back into the street by the computer-controlled opponent

In  Urban Champion , players participate in one-on-one  street fights , competing with opponents to push each other off the sidewalk and past the edge of the screen.

&#91; 6 &#93;

Fighters automatically assume a defensive stance when not attacking, and can raise or lower their arms to shift between blocking and attacking high or blocking and attacking low; the player must strike the area not protected by their opponent's arms to inflict damage and push the opponent back towards the screen's edge.

&#91; 7 &#93;

The player has access to two types of attacks: a weak punch, which does less damage and knockback but is faster and therefore harder to defend against; and a strong punch, which does more damage and knockback but takes longer to perform, making it easier to block, dodge or interrupt.

&#91; 8 &#93;

Battles use a "best 3-of-5 rounds" format.

&#91; 9 &#93;

At the start of each round, both fighters begin with 200 stamina points.

&#91; 9 &#93;

Each punch costs the player one point of stamina to perform, while the weak punch inflicts four points of stamina damage and the strong punch inflicts ten points. A man will occasionally drop flower pots from the windows; if hit by a pot, a fighter will take five points of damage and be temporarily dazed.

&#91; 6 &#93;

&#91; 10 &#93;

In addition, a patrol car may pass by at any point in the fight, which causes the fighters to return to their starting positions.

&#91; 6 &#93;

If a fighter runs out of stamina, they become vulnerable and every attack will inflict the same amount of knockback on them as a strong punch. When a fighter is pushed to the edge of the screen, the round ends as they are knocked off the sidewalk onto the street, and the battle shifts to the next block for the next round.

&#91; 8 &#93;

Each round also features a 99-second timer; if time runs out before the round is complete, the police will arrest the fighter closest to the edge of the screen, making the other player the winner of the round.

&#91; 10 &#93;

When a fighter has lost two rounds, an open  sewer manhole  appears on the street behind them; the player who successfully pushes their opponent into the manhole is declared the winner, briefly celebrating as a woman showers them with  confetti  from a window.

&#91; 8 &#93;

The game has two gameplay modes: the single-player Game A, and the multiplayer Game B.

&#91; 6 &#93;

In Game A, each time the player knocks their opponent into a manhole, a symbol appears in the bottom corner of the screen denoting their victory. The goal is to earn 45 consecutive victories, at which point the player earns the rank of Champion.

Release

[

edit

]

Urban Champion  was inspired by the 1984  Game & Watch  game

Boxing

(also known as

Punch-Out!!  ).

&#91; 8 &#93;

The game was first released in Japan on November 14, 1984, for the  Family Computer ; it was later released in North America and Europe in 1986 for the system's western counterpart, the  Nintendo Entertainment System . An arcade version was released in 1985 under the name  Vs. Urban Champion .

&#91; 11 &#93;

Urban Champion  has been re-released via  emulation  on several later Nintendo consoles. It was first re-released on November 11, 2002 as a set of cards for the  Nintendo e-Reader .

&#91; 12 &#93;

The game was digitally re-released for the  Virtual Console  service, first for the  Wii  in December 2006,

&#91; 8 &#93;

then for the  Wii U  in October 2013.

&#91; 7 &#93;

An updated version for the  Nintendo 3DS  that utilized  autostereoscopic 3D  was developed by  Arika  as part of the

3D Classics

series, and was released on July 13, 2011 in Japan and the rest of the world on August 18.

&#91; 13 &#93;

Hamster Corporation  released the arcade version as part of their

Arcade Archives

series for the  Nintendo Switch  on November 9, 2018.

&#91; 14 &#93;

The NES version was also added to the  Nintendo Classics  service on July 4, 2024.

&#91; 15 &#93;

Reception

[

edit

]

Urban Champion  received largely negative reception. In the special edition  Pak Source  included in the January/February 1990 volume of

Nintendo Power  , which rated all the NES games released in North America from October 1985 to March 1990,  Urban Champion  received (out of 5) scores of 2.5, 2.5, 1.5, and 1.5 for the four categories evaluated.

&#91; 16 &#93;

Along with

Chubby Cherub  , it is one of the only two games to obtain a score below 2 in a category.

&#91; 16 &#93;

Retrospective reviews of the game's Virtual Console release were similarly critical. Levi Buchanan noted it as one of the weakest NES games for the Virtual Console.

&#91; 17 &#93;

GameSpot 's  Jeff Gerstmann  called it tedious when it was originally released, and in the modern day is "about as boring as it can possibly be".

&#91; 6 &#93;

GamePro

described it as a second tier Virtual Console game.

&#91; 18 &#93;

1UP.com 's Jeremy Parish commented that he would rather have nothing at all on the Virtual Console in its debut week than  Urban Champion .

&#91; 19 &#93;

He also cited it as an example of a poor game that gamers would still buy for the e-Reader.

&#91; 20 &#93;

He listed it as one of the worst Virtual Console games.

&#91; 21 &#93;

1UP.com's Patrick Klepek criticized Nintendo for releasing only this and

Baseball

in one week, calling both poor games.

&#91; 22 &#93;

Writer Bob Mackey called it questionable, calling it the "  Bad Street Brawler

of its day".

&#91; 23 &#93;

GameSpy 's Phil Theobald called it "awful", criticizing its cameo in

Tetris DS  .

&#91; 24 &#93;

IGN 's Lucas M. Thomas called it too shallow for an NES game, adding that there was no reason to purchase it.

&#91; 8 &#93;

He also commented that it didn't deserve the distinction of being Nintendo's first head-to-head fighting game.

&#91; 25 &#93;

IGN AU's Cam Shea called it "god awful", and "worse than actually falling down a manhole".

&#91; 26 &#93;

Screw Attack advised that Wii owners not waste their money on it, calling it simple and slow.

&#91; 27 &#93;

However, video game developer Ron Alpert found it to be an acceptable game, calling it one of the simplest of its day, but also an easy game to pick up and play.

&#91; 28 &#93;
